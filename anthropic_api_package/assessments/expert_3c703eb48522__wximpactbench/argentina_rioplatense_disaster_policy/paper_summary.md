### 1. Task Taxonomy / Test Case Categories

WXIMPACTBENCH is described as "the first benchmark for evaluating the capacity of LLMs on disruptive weather impacts" [Q2], structured around two primary evaluation tasks: multi-label classification and ranking-based question answering [Q3, Q15]. The multi-label classification task requires models to simultaneously identify all six societal impact categories present in a given article [Q57], while the ranking-based QA task prompts models to identify the most relevant article from a candidate pool in response to a generated question [Q36]. The six impact categories — Infrastructural, Political, Financial, Ecological, Agricultural, and Human Health — are grounded in prior disaster assessment literature and frameworks [Q11], with detailed definitional guidance provided for each [Q135, Q136, Q137, Q138, Q139, Q140]. The benchmark also evaluates LLMs in both zero-shot and one-shot in-context learning settings [Q113, Q114], and uses topic modeling (LDA over 15 primary weather event types) to structure article selection prior to task construction [Q101].

For the Argentine deployment context, the six top-level categories are broadly applicable to disaster types such as pampero windstorms, Río de la Plata flooding, and ENSO-driven agricultural impacts, but the benchmark does not provide sub-labels or finer granularity within categories [Q11] — a recognized gap for local policy use. Furthermore, the task ontology is built entirely around formal newspaper articles [Q18], with no task types targeting informal social media posts, press releases, or short-form content, which are central to the Argentine deployment's input channels. The benchmark's QA task is also described as a closed-retrieval evaluation, with open-retrieval left explicitly for future work [Q89], further limiting its applicability to scenarios where policy users must assess reliability across a broad and noisy information landscape.

---

### 2. Data Sources and Collection

The benchmark is constructed through a "four-stage well-crafted construction pipeline" [Q1] applied to a corpus of digitized historical newspaper articles. The primary source is identified in one section as three named Canadian newspapers — *La Presse*, *La Patrie*, and the *Montreal Gazette* — obtained through collaboration with the McGill University Library and Archives and the Bibliothèque nationale du Québec [Q97]; a separate section describes the data as obtained through collaboration with a proprietary archive institution that has granted permission to publish a benchmark subset [Q92, Q93]. The data spans two temporal periods to capture linguistic and social change [Q13], and covers 4,018 digitized articles before filtering [Q20]. After topic-aware selection and expert review, the dataset yields 350 high-quality annotated samples in the long-context version and 1,386 in the mixed-context version [Q49, Q121, Q122]. GPT-4O was used to generate pseudo questions for the QA task [Q59], and the candidate pool for ranking was constructed from the same annotated corpus [Q42]. The data is described as publicly available with no privacy concerns [Q94], and the original data was stored as OCR-processed digitized text [Q20].

From an Argentine deployment perspective, the exclusively Canadian, English-language newspaper provenance of this corpus is a significant validity concern along the Input Content dimension. The cultural framing — including which institutions are treated as authoritative, how disaster response is narrated, and the formal journalistic register — reflects North American norms that do not map onto Argentine social media posts, informal press communications, or government bulletins in Rioplatense Spanish. The absence of any Spanish-language or Latin American source material means that the benchmark's data distribution is wholly outside the deployment's primary content domain.

---

### 3. Data Format and Preprocessing

The benchmark's input consists of text-only newspaper articles processed through a four-stage pipeline including OCR error correction, topic-aware selection, and manual annotation [Q19]. Articles exist in two context-length versions: a long-context version averaging approximately 2,987 tokens per article and a mixed-context version averaging approximately 781 tokens per chunk [Q109], with the mixed-context version created by segmenting original articles into approximately 250-token segments [Q46]. Historical articles are noted to employ "more descriptive and elaborate narratives" with "outdated terminology, spelling variations, and evolving writing conventions" [Q14], which differs substantially from modern short-form or social media text. All evaluated models were required to support input lengths of at least 8k tokens [Q55], and the multi-label classification task was administered with a standardized prompt [Q56]; QA pairs were constructed by generating a pseudo question per article using a generative LLM [Q39, Q41]. One-shot examples were handcrafted to feature complex multi-impact samples [Q58], and ranking used a sliding window mechanism to handle long contexts [Q60, Q61]. The token length distribution across both dataset versions is documented in Figure 6 of the paper [Q120].

For the Argentine deployment context, the text-only modality aligns with the deployment's text-based channels, but the register and format diverge meaningfully: the benchmark's inputs are long, formal, historically sourced newspaper narratives [Q83, Q84], whereas the deployment includes short-form social media posts, informal press releases, and potentially noisy modern Spanish-language content. The structured and formal narrative style of historical newspapers is noted as actually making classification easier for LLMs [Q83], which may produce inflated estimates of model capability relative to noisy informal-channel inputs encountered in practice.

---

### 4. Label Categories and Output Types

The benchmark defines six binary impact labels for each article: Infrastructural, Political, Financial, Ecological, Agricultural, and Human Health [Q25, Q103, Q133]. Each label is operationalized as a binary true/false judgment indicating the presence or absence of direct, explicit descriptions of that impact type within the article [Q27, Q117]; annotations are not based on inferred or implied consequences but on explicit textual mentions [Q108]. Detailed classification criteria for each of the six labels are provided in the annotation guidelines and in the LLM prompts, defining, for example, that Human Health is "true" when fatalities, injuries, illness risk, or healthcare access impacts are explicitly mentioned [Q145], and that Political is "true" when governmental decision-making, policy responses, or public discourse around disaster preparedness are mentioned [Q146, Q140]. The multi-label schema allows a single article to carry multiple simultaneous impact labels [Q28], and the formal label representation uses binary vectors of length six per sample [Q33]. The QA task's candidate pool and question targets are derived from the same six-category annotation schema [Q40, Q116].

For the Argentine deployment, the six-label output ontology does not include a credibility, reliability, or signal-to-noise dimension [Q3 — elicitation inference], which is the primary decision-support need for policy users. The deployment envisions credibility scoring as a downstream aggregation layer built on top of these classification outputs, meaning the benchmark's label space functions as a necessary but not sufficient component of the full output pipeline.

---

### 5. Annotation Process

Annotation was conducted by three domain experts following structured guidelines reviewed by meteorological specialists [Q26, Q104, Q105]. The annotators are described as members of a research group "specializing in uncovering the history of a region's climate change through the regional historical weather records" [Q110], whose expertise is said to "ensure the accuracy and reliability of annotations" [Q111]. Topic-aware article selection was performed by researchers specializing in historical climate analysis to reduce temporal and geographic selection bias [Q95]. The annotation task required binary labeling of each of the six impact categories based solely on explicit textual mentions, with annotators instructed to apply meteorological expertise beyond the provided examples [Q107, Q134]. For the mixed-context (chunked) version of the dataset, annotations were conducted independently on each chunk by the same experts rather than inherited from the original article-level labels [Q47, Q48], yielding valuable negative examples. OCR correction quality was validated against human-annotated corrections on a 50-article sample [Q98], and annotation guidelines were formally documented and provided in appendices [Q131]. The overall annotation workflow is described as a structured framework for meteorology experts, with the stated goal of creating a reliable benchmark for LLM evaluation [Q129, Q130].

A significant validity-relevant gap exists in the demographic transparency of annotators: while domain expertise in historical Canadian climate records is clearly stated [Q110], no information is provided about the annotators' cultural familiarity with disaster communication norms outside of the North American/Canadian context. For an Argentine deployment, this matters because annotators' implicit assumptions about what constitutes a "direct" versus "implied" impact (e.g., [Q118]) may not align with how Argentine journalists or government communicators frame the same phenomena. The annotation guidelines' emphasis on "explicit mentions" and "direct impacts rather than implications" [Q147] may systematically disadvantage culturally indirect or government-mediated disaster framing styles that are more common in Argentine media.

---

### 6. Evaluation Metrics and Output Modality

For the multi-label classification task, the benchmark reports F1-score, accuracy, and row-wise accuracy as primary metrics [Q43], with F1 and standard accuracy averaged across the six impact categories, temporal periods, and context lengths [Q44]. Row-wise accuracy is explicitly described as a stricter metric requiring exact multi-label correctness across all six categories for a given article [Q45, Q75], and the paper observes that performance drops dramatically under this stricter evaluation [Q68]. The evaluation also distinguishes between zero-shot and one-shot performance [Q77, Q78] and between historical and modern article subsets [Q81, Q82]. For the ranking-based QA task, standard information retrieval metrics — Hit@1, nDCG@5, Recall@5, and MRR — are used [Q50], and results are reported with temperature set to 0 and averaged over three runs for stability [Q62]. The sliding window mechanism is used to mitigate long-context noise in ranking [Q60]. OCR correction quality is assessed using BLEU and ROUGE scores against human-edited text, yielding high agreement (BLEU 1-gram: 0.9115; ROUGE/L: 0.9438) [Q99, Q100]. Evaluation costs for proprietary models are documented (~$3 for classification, ~$5.5 for QA) [Q126], and open-source evaluations were run on two NVIDIA A6000 GPUs [Q127]. The same classification instructions used for human annotators are embedded in the LLM prompts [Q106].

For the Argentine deployment context, none of the reported metrics directly assess source credibility, misinformation likelihood, or signal reliability — the core output need. The classification and ranking metrics are usable as upstream features for a downstream credibility aggregation workflow, but the output transformation step is non-trivial and is not addressed within the benchmark itself. The paper's finding that one-shot examples offer "limited benefits and might decrease the performance in some cases" [Q78] is relevant for policy users considering prompt engineering strategies when adapting the benchmark to Spanish-language or informal-register content.

### 7. Stated Limitations

The authors acknowledge several categories of limitation directly relevant to validity assessment. On data quality, they note that OCR conversion of historical non-digital newspapers introduces errors from "degraded print quality, inconsistent terminology, and irregular column layouts" [Q23], and that "the lack of high-quality resources constrains the development of comprehensive benchmarks for weather impacts" [Q24]. Processing historical climate text is described as non-trivial due to "period-specific narratives and domain-specific linguistic phenomena" [Q9, Q5], including polysemy requiring distinction between literal and figurative weather-related language [Q10].

On model performance, LLMs are found to consistently struggle with weather impact understanding, particularly for political and ecological categories [Q64, Q65], while financial, agricultural, and human health categories reach only ~55% F1 [Q66], attributed to "abstract and context-dependent narratives" [Q67]. Even with long-context capacity, models benefit from chunk-based context de-noising, but this benefit does not extend to row-wise accuracy [Q72, Q73, Q76]. One-shot in-context learning is found to be largely ineffective, suggesting LLMs "lack sufficient knowledge to disambiguate disruptive weather impacts even with enhanced examples" [Q79].

On benchmark scope and bias, the authors explicitly note potential "biases in underrepresented historical events and linguistic variations" [Q90], acknowledge that "the interpretation of weather-related disruptions in historical newspapers might be influenced by demographic and contextual factors" [Q96], and recognize that "the absence of standardized impact records" constrains vulnerability and resilience assessment [Q102]. A bias risk is flagged when the model used for question generation (GPT-4O) is also evaluated on the QA task [Q88]. The open-retrieval setting for QA is left for future work [Q89], as is expansion to more models and task types [Q91].

For the Argentine deployment, these limitations compound: the benchmark's acknowledged language pattern constraints [Q85] — where historical narrative styles may inflate model performance estimates — directly undermines transfer validity to modern informal Spanish-language content. The absence of sub-national geographic granularity and informal settlement framing (noted in the elicitation as an Argentine policy priority) is not addressed anywhere in the paper's stated limitations, representing an undocumented gap. The computational accessibility note [Q128] is of minor practical relevance to policy users but signals that the benchmark is designed for broader research community use.

Evaluated model APIs include OpenAI (GPT-4O, GPT-4, GPT-3.5-TURBO) [Q123] and DeepSeek [Q124], reflecting a Global North AI ecosystem orientation. No authors with Latin American, Argentine, or Spanish-language NLP affiliations are listed, which is a validity-relevant observation: the benchmark's design, annotation norms, and evaluation assumptions are uniformly grounded in North American academic and institutional contexts, with no representation of the deployment's target cultural or linguistic context.
