### 1. Task Taxonomy / Test Case Categories

MRBench is framed as a unified evaluation framework specifically targeting the student mistake remediation task in mathematics, assessing AI tutor responses along eight pedagogical dimensions grounded in learning science principles [Q1, Q15]. The core task structure is formally defined: given a conversation history between a tutor and student, the benchmark evaluates the appropriateness of the tutor's next response when the student's most recent utterance contains a mistake or confusion [Q12, Q13, Q14]. The eight dimensions are: encouraging active learning [Q17], adapting to student goals and needs [Q18], managing cognitive load and metacognitive skills [Q19], fostering motivation and curiosity [Q20], mistake identification [Q21, Q22], avoiding answer revelation [Q23], providing guidance [Q24], ensuring actionability [Q25], maintaining logical coherence [Q26], sustaining appropriate tutor tone [Q27], and exhibiting human-likeness [Q29]. The taxonomy was deliberately narrowed through iterative analysis to exclude candidate dimensions like grammaticality and empathy, with validation confirming these eight are both necessary and sufficient for the specific task [Q131, Q132]. Across both input and output, the taxonomy explicitly focuses on semantic and pedagogical depth rather than surface attributes [Q91], and the benchmark includes two datasets that differ in mathematical difficulty—elementary operations (Bridge) versus middle-school reasoning (MathDial)—ensuring coverage of both easier and more challenging problem types [Q143, Q144]. From a deployment validity perspective, the taxonomy's focus on general mathematical mistake remediation rather than any particular national curriculum is consistent with the user's confirmation that curriculum-agnostic math errors are sufficient [Q111, Q112]; however, as discussed under Output Content, the operationalization of dimensions like "providing guidance" may carry implicit Western pedagogical assumptions.

---

### 2. Data Sources and Collection

MRBench is assembled from two existing English-language datasets: Bridge [Q44] and MathDial [Q48], both comprising real educational dialogues in the mathematical domain [Q41, Q42, Q43]. The Bridge dataset consists of partial dialogues between real human tutors and students at the elementary level, featuring both novice and expert human tutor responses, with 60 high-quality instances selected from an original pool of 700 [Q44, Q45, Q46]; the key selection criterion was that the student's final utterance exhibit an error or confusion [Q47]. MathDial consists of complete multi-turn conversations between a real human tutor and an LLM acting as a student, grounded in middle-school mathematical reasoning, with 132 instances retained after manual quality inspection [Q48, Q49, Q51]. For the full benchmark, seven state-of-the-art LLMs (GPT-4, Gemini, Sonnet, Mistral, Llama-3.1-8B, Llama-3.1-405B, and Phi3) were prompted to generate responses for all 192 instances, resulting in 1,596 total responses [Q52, Q53]. Critically for the Indian metro deployment context, both source datasets were constructed in Western academic settings where student verbalization of reasoning is extensive [Q95]; Bridge features short, elementary-level dialogue contexts [Q92] while MathDial has longer, more structured exchanges [Q95]. This means the student communication register throughout MRBench—length, explicitness of reasoning, turn structure—reflects Western classroom norms rather than the briefer, more deferential interaction patterns characteristic of Indian students, a gap that is not acknowledged anywhere in the paper. The combination of both datasets is framed as making MRBench "both challenging and comprehensive" [Q97], but this diversity is defined in terms of mathematical difficulty level, not cultural or interactional diversity.

---

### 3. Data Format and Preprocessing

All benchmark data is text-only, consisting of dialogue histories and tutor responses in English [Q133, Q134]. To harmonize formats between the two source datasets, MathDial conversations were truncated at the point where the student makes a mistake, with the next tutor response treated as the expert response—mirroring the partial-conversation structure of Bridge [Q50]. Response and conversation history lengths are measured in characters and differ systematically between the two datasets, with Bridge conversations being shorter and MathDial conversations being longer and more structured [Q141, Q142]; conversation topic metadata is also absent from MathDial [Q145]. The final benchmark comprises 192 dialogue contexts × 9 response sources (7 LLMs + expert human + novice human for Bridge subset) = 1,596 responses [Q54]. LLM responses were generated using a standardized prompt template adapted from Wang et al. (2024a) [Q133, Q134], ensuring consistent input formatting. Length is uniformly estimated via character count rather than tokens or words [Q147]. For the Indian deployment context, the text-only English format matches the deployment environment well and presents no signal-distribution concern; however, the absence of short-turn, low-verbalization student inputs—which would reflect Indian classroom interaction patterns—is a structural limitation in the input content, not the input form.

---

### 4. Label Categories and Output Types

Each of the eight pedagogical dimensions is annotated using a three-tier labeling system, allowing for nuanced distinctions beyond binary correct/incorrect judgments [Q58]. For example, "mistake identification" uses labels of "yes," "to some extent," and "no," where "to some extent" captures genuine ambiguity [Q59, Q60]. The taxonomy specifies a "desired label" for each dimension (detailed in Table 2 and Table 4 of the paper), which serves as the basis for the DAMR scoring metric [Q65, Q128]. One notable complexity in the label scheme is that the desired labels for different dimensions can interact in non-obvious ways: a response can score highly on actionability while scoring poorly on guidance if the guidance provided is factually incorrect [Q84], since only factually correct guidance is treated as useful. The label scheme also collapses "neutral" and "encouraging" tutor tone into a "non-offensive" composite for DAMR purposes, achieving 100% compliance across all tutors on that dimension [Q90]. The taxonomy explicitly focuses on the appropriateness of individual tutor turns rather than the cumulative impact on student learning, which is acknowledged as a scope boundary [Q119]. From a deployment validity standpoint, the three-tier labeling structure is reasonable for the "providing guidance" dimension that Indian teachers prioritize most, but the specific criteria for what counts as "desired" guidance were calibrated against Western pedagogical norms (as reflected in the annotator pool), meaning the label categories may not fully capture the exam-oriented, direct-correction style Indian teachers would consider acceptable.

---

### 5. Annotation Process

The annotation team comprised four individuals—two male and two female—all holding at least a post-graduate degree in Computer Science and proficient in English [Q31]. Notably, the authors explicitly state that direct teaching experience was not required, as the ability to judge responses from the perspective of a student or potential user of AI tutors was deemed sufficient [Q32]. To ensure quality, the authors opted against public crowdsourcing platforms like Prolific or MTurk, instead implementing rigorous in-house training protocols [Q33], including an interactive training document, oral instructions, a structured quiz, and comprehensive annotation guidelines [Q34, Q135, Q136, Q137]. A validation pilot study preceded large-scale annotation: all four annotators independently labeled the same eight dialogues across all eight dimensions, yielding 544 annotations per annotator and a Fleiss' kappa of 0.65, indicating substantial agreement [Q37, Q38, Q39]. For the full MRBench annotation, each annotator covered 48 dialogues, with 40 instances double-annotated for pairwise agreement, yielding an average Cohen's kappa of 0.71 [Q56, Q57, Q61]. LLM-based annotation using Prometheus2 and Llama-3.1-8B was also conducted [Q105], and the resulting correlation scores with human annotations are reported in appendix tables [Q106]. The pilot study also confirmed that annotators found no additional or redundant dimensions needed [Q40], and human responses were evaluated as clearly stratified between novice (ambiguous, short) and expert (more actionable) quality [Q88, Q102].

For the Indian deployment context, the annotator demographics represent a critical validity gap. The team is entirely from a Computer Science background, with no teaching experience required and no mention of familiarity with South Asian pedagogical norms [Q31, Q32]. The institution is based in Abu Dhabi [Q6], and there is no indication that any annotator had exposure to Indian educational contexts, CBSE/NCERT curricula, or the exam-oriented direct-correction style that Indian teachers value. This means the ground-truth "desired labels"—particularly for the "providing guidance" dimension—were calibrated by annotators whose implicit model of good tutoring likely reflects Western or generic academic norms rather than the more directive, exam-readiness-oriented approach Indian professional teachers would endorse. This is the most consequential validity concern for the OC dimension in this deployment.

### 6. Evaluation Metrics and Output Modality

The benchmark employs two primary quantitative metrics: the Desired Annotation Match Rate (DAMR), which measures the percentage of responses receiving the desired annotation label for each dimension [Q64, Q65], and Annotation Correlation (AC), a Pearson's correlation measuring alignment between LLM-generated and human annotations [Q66, Q138]. Human annotations are treated as the gold standard [Q68], and results are reported both overall and broken down by dataset (Bridge and MathDial) [Q149, Q150]. The benchmark evaluates seven LLMs and two human tutor levels (novice and expert) across all eight dimensions, with novice scores reported only on the 60 Bridge instances [Q82, Q83]. Key substantive findings include: GPT-4 reveals answers ~47% of the time, making it a better question-answering system than a tutor [Q69, Q70]; Llama-3.1-405B is the top performer overall [Q99]; Phi3 is the worst, frequently failing contextual understanding [Q72, Q101]; and Llama-3.1-8B performs surprisingly well for its size [Q76, Q77]. Expert human responses score higher than novice but still show weaknesses on some dimensions [Q87]. LLM evaluators (Prometheus2 and Llama-3.1-8B as critics) show predominantly negative correlations with human annotations except on human-likeness [Q107], indicating unreliability for pedagogical dimensions [Q114, Q123]. For the deployment context, the DAMR metric's equal weighting across all eight dimensions may not reflect how Indian professional teachers would actually judge response acceptability — where "providing guidance" is the single most critical dimension [Q3 from elicitation]. The finding that GPT-4 (the deployed model) reveals answers too frequently [Q69] is directly relevant, as Indian teachers focused on exam preparation may view answer revelation as especially problematic.

### 7. Stated Limitations

The authors acknowledge multiple limitations spanning both scope and methodology. On scope, the taxonomy is explicitly limited to student mistake remediation in mathematics and would require verification and likely adaptation for other tasks (concept learning) or subjects [Q117, Q118, Q130]. The annotation captures individual tutor turns rather than overall learning impact, restricting understanding of broader student learning gains [Q120, Q121]. The benchmark's evaluation of LLMs as critics is described as preliminary [Q110], with the authors acknowledging that Prometheus2 was not trained on their taxonomy dimensions, explaining its poor correlation except on human-likeness [Q108, Q109].

Several limitations are particularly salient for the Indian deployment context. The authors note that general NLG metrics fail to capture pedagogical values [Q4, Q8] and that multiple valid ground-truth responses can exist for any given student turn [Q9]—a point that underscores the cultural relativity of what counts as "correct" guidance. The authors also flag that LLMs may generate hallucinations that misguide students and propagate biases [Q124, Q125], which is especially concerning in an exam-oriented educational context where factual accuracy carries high stakes. The interdependencies among dimensions are acknowledged [Q30, Q116], and the paper notes that even incorrect or incomplete guidance can foster student curiosity [Q85]—a pedagogical value more consistent with exploratory Western approaches than with the direct-correction norms Indian teachers favor. The authors broadly conclude that all LLMs and even human tutors lack some pedagogical abilities required for effective tutoring [Q98, Q104], and that the study's insights may help mitigate issues in educational LLM deployment [Q127]. Crucially, the paper does not acknowledge any limitation related to cultural or regional variation in pedagogical norms—a significant gap given that the benchmark is now being considered for deployment in an Indian professional teaching context.
