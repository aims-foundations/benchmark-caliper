## Use Case
An AI system evaluates the reliability and trustworthiness of disaster-related reporting in Argentina by distinguishing verified or informative signals from noisy or unreliable ones across news, social media, and press channels. The primary consumers are policy makers and government agencies in Buenos Aires who need to assess crisis communication quality across multiple information sources.

## Target Population
Argentina, concentrated in Buenos Aires metropolitan area; Rioplatense Spanish speakers with intermediate-to-advanced English; policy makers, emergency management officials, and government analysts; upper-middle-class, master's-degree-or-above education level. Sub-national variation across informal settlement zones (villas), agricultural hinterlands (Pampa, Patagonia), and river basin communities (Río de la Plata) is relevant but not the primary focus.

Are there disaster types (e.g., pampero windstorms, ENSO-driven droughts, urban flash floods in Buenos Aires) whose reporting patterns your users need to evaluate?
A1: The existing benchmark labels are broadly sufficient to cover Argentine disaster types; however, sub-labels or finer-grained categories (e.g., distinguishing specific impact populations or regional hazard types) would be valuable but are absent.

Q2 [IC]: The benchmark's input content is drawn from a specific source culture and media environment that may reflect particular disaster framing, infrastructure norms, and institutional actors. For your Argentine policy makers assessing reporting reliability, would culturally embedded assumptions in such data — such as which agencies are cited as authoritative, what counts as 'normal' disaster response, or how agricultural loss is framed — misalign with what Argentine news sources or government communications typically convey?
A2: Cultural embedding is not expected to be problematic for formal press and news articles, but social media and informal content channels likely carry meanings, norms, and communicative conventions not well represented in a benchmark drawn from a different cultural context.

Q3 [OO]: Your deployment centers on distinguishing noisy or unreliable signals from verified disaster reporting — a reliability or trustworthiness judgment — rather than simply classifying impact type or content type. Does your use case require a scoring dimension for source credibility, misinformation likelihood, or signal-to-noise quality, which may not map onto the benchmark's output space and scoring dimensions?
A3: Source credibility scoring is the ideal output, but the user envisions building this on top of the benchmark's existing classification outputs by combining model results with external metadata (e.g., source popularity, institutional affiliation) — so the benchmark's output taxonomy is treated as a necessary but insufficient foundation rather than a direct mismatch.

Q4 [OC]: The benchmark's ground-truth labels were likely produced by annotators familiar with disaster communication norms from a different cultural context than your deployment. For your context, would Argentine policy experts, emergency management officials, or journalists plausibly disagree with those labels — for instance, on whether a given report constitutes a 'real signal' of disaster impact versus noise — especially given differences in how Argentine media covers government response or social vulnerability?
A4: Argentine crisis communication norms are less institutionally settled than in North America/Canada; however, Argentine policy professionals tend to draw on international (Global North) standards as reference points, and local press broadly follows those conventions. Consequently, label disagreement with benchmark annotations derived from that normative context is expected to be limited, though the more fluid Argentine public discourse may produce edge cases.

## Flagged Gaps

1. **Social media content from Latin American / Argentine context**: The benchmark consolidates English-language tweets from global disaster events with no apparent representation of Spanish-language or Argentine social media discourse. Downstream search should investigate whether any CrisisBench constituent datasets include Spanish-language content or Latin American disaster events, and whether the informal communicative conventions of Argentine Twitter/X (e.g., political satire, colloquial Rioplatense, institutional credibility cues) are present in the training distribution.

2. **Sub-label granularity for Argentine hazard types**: Users identified that finer-grained categories (pampero events, ENSO-drought cycles, Río de la Plata flooding, villa-impact framing) are absent. Search should assess whether humanitarian classification schemes used in CrisisBench (e.g., ISCRAM-derived taxonomies) have been extended or adapted for South American or Ibero-American disaster contexts.

3. **Source credibility / reliability scoring dimension**: The benchmark does not natively score trustworthiness or misinformation likelihood. Search should investigate whether any post-2021 extensions to CrisisBench or adjacent crisis-NLP benchmarks (e.g., CREDBANK, CrisisMMD, TREC-IS) have introduced credibility or veracity dimensions that could close this gap for the deployment.

4. **Annotator population and label provenance**: The ground-truth labels in the constituent CrisisBench datasets likely originate from crowdsourced or academic annotators in North American/European contexts. Search should identify the specific annotation protocols, worker demographics, and inter-annotator agreement figures for the eight constituent datasets to assess how well they represent Argentine institutional and journalistic judgment.

5. **Cross-channel generalization (news vs. social media)**: The benchmark is exclusively tweet-derived; the deployment spans news articles, press releases, and social media. Search should investigate whether CrisisBench's classification models generalize to non-tweet text formats and whether any transfer or domain-adaptation studies exist for press/news disaster content.
