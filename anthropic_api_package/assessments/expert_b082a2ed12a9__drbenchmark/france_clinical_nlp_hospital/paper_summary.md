### 1. Task Taxonomy / Test Case Categories

DrBenchmark is presented as the first large French biomedical language understanding benchmark, comprising 20 diverse downstream tasks [Q83, Q84]. The task types span part-of-speech (POS) tagging, named-entity recognition (NER), multi-class and multi-label classification, intent classification, multiple-choice question answering (MCQA), and semantic textual similarity (STS) [Q1, Q4, Q18], with the explicit goal of including both classical tasks like NER and POS and more challenging tasks like MCQA and multi-label classification [Q20]. Individual datasets contribute varied subtasks: for example, DEFT-2020 supports both sentence-pair similarity scoring and sentence retrieval selection [Q21, Q22, Q23]; FrenchMedMCQA contributes two MCQA subtasks focused on identifying correct answers and counting correct answers in a five-choice format [Q39]; and PxCorpus contributes spoken-language intent classification across four classes and a 38-class NER task derived from medical drug prescription transcripts [Q44]. Aggregated results are reported across five designated categories — POS, NER, MCQA, MCC, MLC, and STS — enabling task-family-level performance analysis [Q81].

From an **input ontology (IO)** validity perspective, this taxonomy is a significant concern for the target pharmaceutical regulatory deployment. The 20 tasks draw predominantly from scientific literature, clinical cases, and clinical trial protocols, with only the Mantra-GSC patents subset and ESSAI clinical trials offering any proximity to regulatory document genres [Q40, Q43]. There is no coverage of EU regulatory submission document types — SmPCs, patient information leaflets (PILs), or CTD modules — which are structurally formulaic and legally constrained in ways that differ substantially from clinical or research prose. The QUAERO corpus (sourced from EMEA drug leaflets and MEDLINE biomedical titles) [Q33, Q34] represents the closest genre match to regulatory labeling, but it is used only for NER and does not include STS tasks calibrated to regulatory equivalence judgments. The absence of tasks specifically designed around EMA/ANSM regulatory genres means the benchmark's input ontology covers only a subset of what the pharmaceutical compliance deployment requires.

---

### 2. Data Sources and Collection

DrBenchmark assembles data from a diverse range of origins, including scientific literature, clinical trials, clinical cases, and speech transcriptions [Q19]. Existing French biomedical NLP resources are incorporated, including the QUAERO French Medical Corpus (sourced from EMEA drug leaflets and MEDLINE biomedical titles, totalling 103,056 words) [Q33, Q34]; E3C (multilingual clinical cases, French subset retained) [Q29, Q31]; DEFT-2021 (275 clinical cases from the 2019 DEFT challenge) [Q24]; MorFITT (3,624 PMC Open Access biomedical abstracts) [Q38]; Mantra-GSC (biomedical abstracts, drug labels, and patents from a multilingual NER dataset, French subset only) [Q40]; ESSAI (7,247 clinical trial protocols) [Q43]; and CAS (3,790 clinical cases) [Q42]. One dataset — DiaMed — was created specifically for DrBenchmark, comprising 739 clinical cases collected from the Pan African Medical Journal open-access journal [Q45]. Benchmark authors note that dataset licensing remains unchanged from the original sources and that DrBenchmark does not redistribute datasets [Q96].

From an **input content (IC)** validity standpoint, none of the identified data sources appear to contain text originating from French overseas territories, and the Pan African Medical Journal source for DiaMed, while potentially containing tropical pathology cases, is not described as targeting French overseas territory populations specifically [Q45]. The regulatory deployment context requires entity types such as international nonproprietary names (INNs), ATC codes, excipient nomenclature, and EMA-template posology expressions; these are not represented in the described data sources beyond QUAERO's limited EMEA drug leaflet coverage [Q34]. The note that reference benchmarks BLURB, BLUE, and CBLUE predominantly serve English and Chinese [Q12] underscores DrBenchmark's value for French, but does not resolve the gap between research/clinical source genres and pharmaceutical regulatory document genres required for the deployment.

---

### 3. Data Format and Preprocessing

DrBenchmark is exclusively text-based, processing French biomedical text inputs without audio, image, or cross-script complexity [Q31, Q48]. For datasets lacking predefined train/validation/test splits, the authors applied a consistent 70/10/20 random split (as for E3C and Mantra-GSC) [Q32, Q40], and CAS and ESSAI received the same treatment [Q42, Q43]. A practical toolkit built on HuggingFace Datasets, Transformers, and PyTorch is provided, including normalized data loaders, predefined splits, and pre-training and evaluation scripts [Q48]. Tokenization is highlighted as a structural consideration: various tokenization algorithms are employed across models [Q90], and the influence of tokenizers was investigated by examining the average number of sub-tokens produced per word [Q89]. Experiments also examined fine-tuning under limited data conditions by sampling 25%, 50%, 75%, and 100% of training data to assess data efficiency [Q68]. One preprocessing decision with validity implications is the handling of QUAERO's nested entities: only higher-granularity annotations were retained (losing an average of 6.06% of EMEA annotations and 8.90% of MEDLINE annotations), and long EMEA documents exceeding typical model input length limits were sentence-split [Q37].

### 4. Label Categories and Output Types

The label space across DrBenchmark's tasks is extensive and domain-specific. POS tagging uses fine-grained French morphosyntactic tagsets: CAS employs 31 POS classes [Q42, Q101] and ESSAI uses 41 POS tags [Q43, Q102]. NER label sets include QUAERO's 10 UMLS Semantic Group categories [Q35, Q103]; E3C's clinical entity and temporal/factuality annotation layers [Q30, Q104]; Mantra-GSC's Medline (11 classes) and EMEA/Patents (10 classes) schemes [Q106]; and DEFT-2021's 13 fine-grained entity types including anatomy, pathology, substance, and treatment [Q28, Q108]. Multi-label classification in DiaMed uses 22 ICD-10 chapter-level classes [Q45, Q107], while DEFT-2021 multi-label classification uses 23 MeSH Chapter C axes [Q27]. MorFITT uses 12 medical specialty labels [Q38, Q105], and STS tasks use continuous similarity scores from 0 to 5 [Q22, Q41].

From an **output ontology (OO)** validity perspective, the STS scoring rubric is a critical concern for regulatory compliance deployment. The benchmark's STS tasks use a coarse 0–5 Likert-style scale representing general semantic proximity [Q22, Q41, Q54], which does not distinguish legally significant small-magnitude differences — such as altered dose thresholds or contraindicated population qualifiers — from semantically proximate but legally equivalent phrasings. EMA/ANSM regulatory equivalence judgments would require a scoring function sensitive to exactly such fine-grained distinctions, and there is no evidence in the label schema that such calibration exists. For NER, the entity categories (UMLS Semantic Groups, ICD-10 chapters, MeSH axes) reflect clinical and research ontologies rather than regulatory labeling ontologies (INNs, ATC codes, EMA posology templates), which represents a systematic category mismatch for the deployment's compliance-checking function. The ICD-10 chapter-level labels in DiaMed are explicitly described as coarse [Q45], further limiting their regulatory granularity.

### 5. Annotation Process

Annotation practices vary across the constituent datasets. DEFT-2021 was manually annotated for both multi-label classification and NER [Q25]. CLISTER's 1,000 sentence pairs were manually annotated by multiple annotators who assigned 0–5 similarity scores, subsequently averaged into a single floating-point reference score [Q41]. DiaMed — the benchmark's only purpose-built dataset — was manually annotated by several annotators including one medical expert, applying 22 ICD-10 chapter-level labels to 739 clinical cases from an open-access journal; inter-annotator agreement was computed across two sessions of 15 clinical cases each using Cohen's Kappa and Gwet's AC1 [Q45, Q46]. CAS's POS annotations were produced automatically via the Tagex 3 tool and validated against manual annotations, achieving 98% precision [Q42], representing a silver-standard rather than gold-standard annotation process.

From an **output content (OC)** validity perspective, the annotation workforce is predominantly composed of clinical professionals and NLP researchers — not regulatory affairs specialists or legal experts. The DiaMed medical expert provides clinical grounding [Q45], but no annotator with regulatory affairs expertise (e.g., familiarity with EMA SmPC guidelines, ANSM circulars, or CTD documentation standards) is identified across any of the datasets. This means that ground-truth labels reflect clinical and research annotation norms, which may systematically diverge from the regulatory-legal standards that constitute the authoritative ground truth in the pharmaceutical compliance deployment. The silver-standard POS annotations in CAS [Q42] further underscore that label authority is not uniformly adjudicated gold-standard across the benchmark, a concern that compounds the OC validity gap for high-stakes regulatory use.

### 6. Evaluation Metrics and Output Modality

DrBenchmark evaluates 8 state-of-the-art pre-trained masked language models (MLMs) using a consistent fine-tuning protocol with the same hyperparameters across tasks; reported results are averaged over four runs, with statistical significance assessed via Student's t-test [Q2, Q6, Q51]. Sequence labeling tasks (NER, POS) are scored using SeqEval with IOB2 format, configured to predict only the label on the first sub-token of each word, providing tokenizer-agnostic evaluation [Q52, Q53]. STS tasks are scored using both Spearman correlation and the mean relative solution distance accuracy (EDRM) metric as defined by DEFT-2020's original authors [Q54]. Classification tasks are compared against a majority-class baseline [Q56]. The best model is saved based on validation metric performance to mitigate overfitting [Q100], and hyperparameter details are provided in an appendix for reproducibility [Q49, Q99].

Performance aggregation reveals that domain- and language-specialized biomedical models lead on up to 75% of tasks [Q62], with DrBERT-FS achieving top performance on 8 tasks and DrBERT-CP on 5 [Q61]. Generalist models lead on only 4 of 20 tasks but remain competitive on most [Q64]. Data efficiency experiments using 25–100% training subsets were conducted with four runs per configuration, keeping validation and test sets fixed [Q69, Q70, Q72]. From an **output form (OF)** validity standpoint, the benchmark outputs continuous scores (STS) and discrete labels (NER, classification) consistent with the deployment system's multi-candidate confidence-score design; however, the benchmark does not specify or evaluate confidence calibration, uncertainty thresholds for human review triggers, or the granularity at which confidence scores are reported, which are all operationally significant for the human-in-the-loop adjudication workflow described in the deployment context [Q82].

### 7. Stated Limitations

The authors explicitly acknowledge that French biomedical NLP resources are limited compared to English and Chinese, and that no prior large French biomedical benchmark existed before DrBenchmark [Q5, Q9, Q16]. They further note that general benchmarks may inadequately evaluate in-domain models and that most biomedical benchmarks focus on a narrow set of languages [Q10, Q11]. Empirically, the key finding is that no single model excels across all tasks [Q3, Q57], and several biomedical tasks exhibit relatively poor performance even with specialized models [Q86]; the authors postulate that MLMs may not be optimal for tasks such as QA or multi-label classification [Q87]. Model-behavior findings include the observation that French biomedical models (DrBERT-FS, DrBERT-CP, CamemBERT-bio) tend to lead on most tasks [Q60], but the cross-lingual generalist XLM-RoBERTa is competitive without reaching first place [Q59], and CamemBERT-based models struggle in low-resource corpora like MantraGSC Patents [Q73].

Preprocessing limitations include annotation loss from nested entity simplification in QUAERO (6.06–8.90%) and document segmentation for long EMEA texts [Q37]. The tokenization analysis yields the counter-intuitive finding that higher segmentation (DrBERT-CP) outperforms lower segmentation (FlauBERT) across 16 of 20 tasks, leading the authors to conclude that tokenization plays a minor role [Q77, Q79, Q80]. The authors also acknowledge that pre-training data size — especially the amount of specialized biomedical data — has not been thoroughly investigated [Q91], and that the computational cost was substantial (~2,500 GPU hours, ~36.9 kgCO2eq) [Q92, Q93, Q94]. Importantly for the deployment context, there is no stated limitation addressing the absence of regulatory document genres, overseas-territory linguistic variation, or EMA/ANSM-specific annotation standards — gaps that the benchmark's authors do not flag but that are material to the pharmaceutical compliance use case [Q85, Q88].
