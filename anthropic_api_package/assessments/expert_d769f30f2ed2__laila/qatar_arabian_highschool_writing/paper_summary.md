### 1. Task Taxonomy / Test Case Categories

LAILA's benchmark is organized around two main experimental setups — prompt-specific and cross-prompt — each addressing distinct research questions about how models compare in overall performance and across individual traits [Q14, Q46]. The test cases are drawn from 8 prompts spanning only two genres: 3 explanatory and 5 persuasive [Q38], a deliberate but narrow genre scope that the paper acknowledges [Q89]. For a deployment targeting Arab high-school students across multiple countries, this genre restriction is a significant validity concern: literary analysis, religious text commentary, and cultural commentary — all common in Arab curricula — are entirely absent [Q125], and the benchmark's rubric was designed specifically for persuasive/argumentative writing [Q118].

Model evaluation spans three architectural families: feature-based (FB) models including LR, RF, XGB, and NN [Q49]; encoder-based models including AraBERT with handcrafted features and AraT5 [Q50]; and Arabic-centric LLMs (ALLaM, Command-R7B-Arabic, and Fanar) evaluated under zero-shot and few-shot settings [Q51]. LLM evaluation is structured around both zero-shot and few-shot prompting paradigms [Q180], with the zero-shot setting requiring JSON-formatted trait-score outputs [Q181] and few-shot examples selected either from within the same prompt (prompt-specific) or from different prompts (cross-prompt) [Q187, Q188]. The AES task is also formulated as a text generation problem for the AraT5 model, predicting trait scores sequentially [Q147], and ProTACT and MOOSE — originally English AES architectures — are adapted for Arabic [Q164, Q171]. Critically, none of the benchmarked models generate natural-language revision suggestions; the entire taxonomy validates numeric scoring only, creating a direct mismatch with the deployment's need for actionable, explanatory formative feedback [Q3 in elicitation; see also Q48].

The benchmark's nine design principles [Q20] guided collection toward authenticity [Q21], diversity [Q22], and gender balance [Q23], which are input-side taxonomy choices. However, the output-side task taxonomy — predicting holistic and trait scores jointly in a multitask setup [Q48] — is entirely numeric, meaning the benchmark cannot validate whether any model can explain *why* a student lost points on a given trait dimension in pedagogically actionable language.

### 2. Data Sources and Collection

LAILA is described as the largest publicly available Arabic AES dataset to date, comprising 7,859 essays across 8 prompts written by 4,372 high-school students in Qatar [Q1, Q2, Q80]. Data collection was conducted under IRB approval through 27 school visits (24 initial, three follow-up) across one academic year, with each visit lasting approximately six hours and staffed by at least two team members alongside IT and proctoring teachers [Q3, Q27, Q28]. Essays were written under exam-style supervised conditions, with participation entirely voluntary and independent of students' academic grades [Q96], and prompts were carefully designed to be developmentally appropriate, culturally relevant to the Arabic-speaking context, and free of sensitive content [Q99].

The dataset was designed with an explicit goal of diversity — drawing from multiple schools and student populations with varied academic and demographic backgrounds, with prompts varied across genres to promote cultural relevance and model generalization [Q22]. Gender balance was also deliberately pursued to reduce gender-based bias [Q23, Q100]. The dataset includes 3,446 explanatory and 4,413 persuasive essays [Q38], and essay lengths average 171 words [Q133]. Compared with English resources (ASAP/ASAP++, TOEFL11, ELLIPSE, PERSUADE) [Q17] and other non-English datasets (ACEA, TCFLE-8, MERLIN) [Q18], LAILA represents a meaningful advance, and the authors position it as the first large-scale publicly available Arabic AES resource [Q79].

For the deployment context, the critical limitation is geographic: all essays originate from a single country, Qatar, and grades 10–12 [Q88]. While the authors note partial mitigation through Qatar's large Arab expatriate population representing diverse dialects and backgrounds [Q88], this is an inferential claim without empirical validation. LLM selection was also data-source-driven, guided by the Open Arabic LLM Leaderboard [Q178], and referenced datasets like ZaQQ, ELLIPSE, PERSUADE, MERLIN, and TOEFL11 situate LAILA within a broader comparative landscape [Q109–Q113].

### 3. Data Format and Preprocessing

LAILA essays are text-only Arabic, written digitally on Microsoft Forms under a 65-minute timed constraint [Q25, Q26], which aligns well with the deployment's text-based interaction model — there is no modality mismatch between benchmark and deployment (LOWER priority dimension). Essay lengths show a unimodal distribution centered at approximately 171 words, with the peak concentration between 150 and 180 words, and very few essays exceeding 500 words [Q134, Q135, Q138]. Shorter essays (11–60 words, totaling 1,061) are also represented, with a minimum of 11 words resulting from the cleaning step that excluded essays of 10 words or fewer [Q136, Q137].

Data cleaning involved resolving duplicate and misidentified submissions, removing submissions lacking meaningful content, and excluding essays with 10 or fewer words [Q29]. Personally identifiable information was systematically removed, and pre-generated anonymous identifiers were used to protect participant privacy [Q97]. For model input, 816 handcrafted features spanning surface, readability, lexical, semantic, and syntactic properties were implemented [Q54], applied to FB models, ProTACT, MOOSE, and AraBERT [Q55], with feature selection based on Pearson and Spearman correlations [Q56] — though MOOSE did not use explicit feature selection [Q57]. An English-translated version of the CAST rubric is also provided for reference [Q116].

Model-specific input formats vary considerably: AraT5 receives an instruction plus essay text and generates trait names and scores sequentially, with prediction order aligned to the rubric to mirror human annotator scoring order [Q148, Q149]; AraBERT uses max-pooled token representations concatenated with handcrafted features [Q157]; ProTACT uses CNN and LSTM over POS embeddings with AraVec replacing GloVe for Arabic adaptation [Q165, Q167], and POS embeddings extracted via Camel Tools [Q168]; MOOSE uses AraBERTv02 for tokenization and AraBERTv01 for scoring [Q173]; and LLMs operate with context limited to 4,096 tokens with 5-shot examples, iteratively removing examples when the prompt exceeds this limit [Q184, Q185]. Output activations use sigmoid rescaled to per-trait ranges [Q159], and LLM outputs are enforced in JSON format via the outlines library [Q190].

### 4. Label Categories and Output Types

LAILA's label schema is built on 7 writing traits: Relevance (REL), Organization (ORG), Vocabulary (VOC), Style (STY), Development (DEV), Mechanics (MEC), and Grammar (GRA) [Q34, Q115], alongside a Holistic (HOL) score computed as the sum of the 7 trait scores [Q35]. Six traits (all except REL) are rated on a 6-point scale (0–5), while REL uses a narrower 3-point scale (0 = not relevant, 1 = partially relevant, 2 = fully relevant) [Q36, Q120]. A special rule applies: if REL = 0, all other trait scores are set to 0, as irrelevant essays are not subject to further evaluation [Q37], and a score of 0 is also assigned for below-minimum performance or off-topic content [Q117].

Score distributions across the 8 prompts show that most traits approximate a normal distribution [Q119], with 86% of essays receiving the highest REL score of 2 [Q40]. Grammar and vocabulary show the highest frequencies of score 1 (16% each), followed by Mechanics at 13%, identifying these as the most challenging dimensions for students [Q40]. The label schema is grounded in the CAST rubric developed by Qatar University Testing Center (QUTC) [Q33], which provides Arabic-language descriptors for each trait level [Q114]. For deployment validity, this is a key concern: the label schema produces numeric ordinal scores, not natural-language explanations [Q108], meaning the benchmark validates a scoring function that is qualitatively different from the formative feedback function the deployment requires. There is no label category in LAILA for revision suggestions, explanation quality, or pedagogical actionability.

### 5. Annotation Process

The annotation team comprised 6 annotators and 3 supervisors, all Arabic language teachers or lecturers, with five holding advanced degrees (MSc or PhD) in the Arabic language [Q30, Q31]. Supervisors oversaw training, quality assurance, and dispute resolution, while annotators performed primary scoring [Q32]; annotators were qualified Arabic language educators compensated at or above local professional rates [Q101]. Annotation was conducted on the Assessment Gourmet Platform, which enforced annotator blindness to student identity and demographic information [Q42, Q98], with essay distribution randomized to minimize systematic bias and session lengths capped to prevent fatigue [Q43].

Annotation guidelines were developed by 2 supervisors, including detailed terminology, exemplars, and annotated practices per prompt type; annotators completed training and moderation sessions before formal annotation to harmonize rubric interpretations [Q41, Q6]. Each essay was independently scored by two annotators; when HOL score differences were less than 6 points, the mean rounded down was adopted as the final score; discrepancies of ≥ 6 points were escalated to a supervisor for adjudication and annotator feedback [Q43]. For reproducibility, the full data collection and annotation process was documented and shared [Q82], and IRB approval was obtained with informed consent from students and legal guardians [Q95]. The CAST rubric used was provided in Arabic by QUTC [Q114], anchoring the annotation in Qatar-specific institutional standards — a relevant OC-dimension consideration for deployment across other Arab national curricula.

Inter-annotator agreement was measured using Quadratic Weighted Kappa (QWK), with substantial agreement across prompts (average QWK ranging from 0.66 for P5 to 0.75 for P1) [Q44]. Notably, P5 showed the lowest agreement and the highest rate of third-annotator adjudication (23.3%), suggesting that some prompts generate more interpretive disagreement [Q45]. Structured training, moderation, and adjudication procedures were applied consistently [Q102], and the Assessment Gourmet platform was provided by Advanced Group for Information Technology (AGI) [Q85].

### 6. Evaluation Metrics and Output Modality

The primary evaluation metric throughout is Quadratic Weighted Kappa (QWK), used both for inter-annotator agreement [Q44] and for model-vs-human score agreement [Q58]. Two experimental setups are reported: prompt-specific (5-fold cross-validation with 70/10/20 training/dev/test splits, stratified by prompt) [Q52] and cross-prompt [Q14]. Hyperparameters were tuned via Bayesian optimization using Optuna's TPESampler with 20 trials and a fixed random seed [Q59, Q60], and final model selection was based on average QWK on the development set [Q61].

Key performance findings include: AraBERT achieves the best prompt-specific performance (average QWK: 0.74, approaching human IAA) [Q70], but drops sharply in the cross-prompt setup (average QWK: 0.549) [Q71]; MOOSE achieves the highest cross-prompt average (QWK: 0.597) [Q73]; FB models remain competitive in cross-prompt settings, confirming that handcrafted features capture prompt-independent linguistic properties [Q74]; and LLMs show high sensitivity to prompting strategy, with Fanar and R7B improving substantially under few-shot conditions (+19 and +11.6 points respectively) while ALLaM drops 7 points [Q65]. No single model consistently outperforms others across all individual traits [Q66], with MOOSE leading on REL, STY, and GRA [Q67], and different models leading on VOC, ORG, DEV, and MEC [Q68].

Score distributions confirm that most traits show near-normal distributions stable across prompts [Q121], though REL scores are consistently skewed toward the maximum [Q123], and ORG, VOC, and STY distributions peak at 3 [Q127, Q130]. Holistic score was computed as the sum of trait scores for LLM experiments [Q182], and LLM experiments were repeated three times with different random seeds to account for example-selection variability [Q189]. Experiments were run on NVIDIA RTX A6000 and NVIDIA A10 GPU hardware [Q140]. For the deployment context, QWK measures alignment between model and human numeric scores — it cannot capture whether model outputs would be pedagogically useful as formative feedback, which is the actual deployment output function.

### 7. Stated Limitations

The authors explicitly acknowledge the data scarcity problem in Arabic AES, noting that existing Arabic resources are small in scale, lack trait-specific annotations, or consist solely of unannotated essays [Q10, Q11], situating LAILA as a necessary but partial corrective [Q19]. The most significant limitation for the target deployment is geographic scope: LAILA was collected exclusively in Qatar from grades 10–12, which may limit generalizability to other Arabic-speaking populations given diverse national educational systems [Q88]. The authors offer partial mitigation via Qatar's Arab expatriate population representing diverse dialects [Q88], but explicitly acknowledge this does not fully resolve the concern — a finding directly relevant to the deployment's intended pan-Arab reach.

Genre coverage is a second documented limitation: LAILA includes only explanatory and persuasive prompts, restricting model robustness for narrative, descriptive, literary, religious, or cultural commentary genres [Q89] — precisely the genres flagged as deployment gaps. Two prompts (P3 and P4) have fewer essays than others, potentially introducing evaluation imbalance [Q90]. The narrow REL scoring scale (0–2) may cause QWK to underrepresent subtle model prediction differences [Q91], and the average essay length of only 171 words restricts assessment of models on long-form academic tasks [Q92]. Prompt-level variation in score distributions and annotator agreement raises additional concerns: P1 and P5 (both explanatory) showed higher frequencies of REL = 0, suggesting potential prompt ambiguity [Q124, Q126], and P5's lower IAA [Q45] suggests that explanatory prompts may be inherently harder to score consistently.

The authors also caution that the prompt-specific setup has limited practical applicability due to its reliance on labeled target-prompt essays [Q72], and emphasize that cross-prompt AES — more realistic but with all models still far from human performance — should be the priority for future research [Q77]. The dataset is explicitly restricted to research and educational use, prohibiting high-stakes individual assessment decisions [Q103, Q104], with users required to prevent re-identification, avoid demographic profiling, and refrain from operational deployment without independent validation [Q105]. Fairness auditing and stakeholder consultation are recommended before any real-world application [Q106]. The authors also note that benchmarking results do not exhaustively cover SOTA AES models and serve only as baselines [Q93], with the primary contribution being the dataset itself rather than novel model architectures [Q94]. Minor implementation notes also flag that certain hyperparameter configurations were chosen to mitigate training instabilities specific to the dataset [Q163], and that fixing few-shot examples at 5 for fair LLM comparison, despite Command-R7B supporting a larger context, was a deliberate but constraining design choice [Q186].

The entirely Qatar-based institutional ecosystem — funders, rubric developers, annotators, and schools — is a positive signal for alignment with Qatar-specific deployment but also confirms that no cross-national validation of annotation norms or rubric conventions has been performed, which is a material gap for the broader Arab-world deployment scenario.
