### 1. Task Taxonomy / Test Case Categories

FOLIO defines its primary task as natural language reasoning with first-order logic: given a set of premises defining a "world," models must determine the truth value of given conclusions [Q15]. A secondary task — NL-FOL translation — is proposed alongside the main reasoning task, requiring models to translate natural language stories into their equivalent FOL representations [Q3, Q18, Q67]. This dual-task structure means FOLIO assesses both logical inference from NL premises and the structural translation between NL and formal logic, making it more demanding than benchmarks that test only surface-level entailment [Q7].

In terms of logical complexity, FOLIO is explicitly designed to exceed prior datasets: the modal reasoning depth reaches five or more hops for 28.7% of examples [Q60], and the dataset contains far more distinct abstract syntax trees than predecessors such as RuleTaker and LogicNLI [Q61]. The benchmark incorporates syllogism structures [Q41, Q43], including all 24 valid syllogism types plus conjunction, disjunction, and implication operators, and evaluates models across fine-tuning (BERT, RoBERTa) [Q84] and few-shot prompting regimes covering LLaMA, GPT-NeoX, GPT-3, GPT-3.5-Turbo, and GPT-4 [Q88], as well as advanced prompting strategies like chain-of-thought, self-consistency, and tree-of-thought [Q90, Q91].

From a deployment-validity perspective, the task ontology has a critical structural limitation for the target use case. FOLIO tests reasoning over fully self-contained, explicitly stated premise sets [Q15, Q64], whereas the engineering deployment requires detecting conflicts that depend on premises drawn from externally referenced standards (MIL-STDs, IEEE specifications) that are never restated in the document under analysis. FOLIO contains no analog for open-world or implicit-premise reasoning. The single-context, single-story structure [Q15] is also fundamentally mismatched against the deployment's cross-document requirement-tracing scenario, which is the central use case [Q4 elicitation]. Expert human annotators familiar with FOL achieve 95.98% on FOLIO [Q111], confirming the task is well-posed for closed-world logical reasoning — but this does not transfer to reasoning where relevant world-knowledge must be retrieved or inferred from domain conventions absent from the premise set.

### 2. Data Sources and Collection

FOLIO's 1,430 examples are drawn from two collection pipelines [Q1, Q32, Q33]. The first, WikiLogic, seeds human annotators with randomly selected Wikipedia articles, which are used as inspiration for writing entirely new stories from scratch — not copied text — resulting in 304 stories with a vocabulary that accounts for 74% of the dataset's total 4,351 words [Q34, Q38, Q58, Q62]. This grounding in real-world, human-written narrative is explicitly intended to distinguish FOLIO from prior datasets that are purely synthetic and template-based [Q14].

The second pipeline, HybLogic, uses a hybrid approach combining machine-generated syllogism templates with human annotation: logical story templates are generated by chaining valid syllogisms, and human annotators then populate abstract placeholders with real-world nouns and phrases [Q39, Q40, Q42, Q44]. HybLogic contributes 183 stories [Q58]. A notable downstream observation is that LLMs may have encountered WikiLogic-style texts in pretraining, introducing potential data-contamination confounds [Q104], while RoBERTa fine-tuned on HybLogic outperforms on that subset because template patterns are learnable [Q105]. The total annotation effort was 980 man-hours across six development stages [Q32]. For the deployment context, both collection pipelines produce domain-neutral, general-knowledge prose — neither reflects the specialized, normative vocabulary of defense or aerospace specification documents (e.g., "shall," ICD conventions, MIL-STD references), which is a systematic gap in input content coverage.

---

### 3. Data Format and Preprocessing

FOLIO's input format is natural language prose stories, each consisting of n premises and m conclusions stored as parallel NL and FOL pairs [Q64, Q65]. Stories are written from scratch without templates (in the WikiLogic track) [Q35], or from syllogism-derived templates (HybLogic) [Q45], and annotators are required to write parallel FOL sentences for all premises and conclusions [Q37]. The FOL syntax follows the conventions of Russell and Norvig (2010) as standard in the AI community [Q52], using negation, conjunction, disjunction, implication, universal and existential quantifiers, and equality [Q138].

Naturalness and grammatical correctness are enforced through multiple preprocessing steps: all sentences are checked with Grammarly [Q49], reviewed by annotators with English Literature backgrounds [Q50], and screened for natural language ambiguity [Q51]. Specific linguistic conventions are standardized — for instance, "either-or" for exclusive disjunction [Q130], "A or B, or both" for inclusive disjunction [Q131], and preferred phrasings for universal statements [Q132, Q133, Q134]. The dataset is split 70%/15%/15% by story (train 1,001 / validation 203 / test 226) to prevent test-set leakage of premises seen during training [Q78, Q79].

For the deployment context, the format is text-only English with no modality mismatch — both benchmark and deployment operate on English prose [Q22]. However, FOLIO's premise sets are short, self-contained narratives with ambiguity actively eliminated [Q51], whereas engineering specifications are long-form, multi-section documents with highly normalized language ("shall/should"), cross-references, and deliberate ambiguity in some requirements. The distribution of FOL ASTs and readability scores [Q154, Q106] characterizes a dataset optimized for logical variety in compact stories, not for the multi-thousand-word, multi-document contexts that the deployment must handle.

---

### 4. Label Categories and Output Types

FOLIO employs a three-way label taxonomy for the reasoning task: **True**, **False**, and **Unknown** for each conclusion given its premise set [Q36, Q66]. The correctness of these labels is grounded in formal FOL semantics: all examples carry FOL annotations that are automatically verified by an FOL inference engine, ensuring that labels reflect provable logical truth values rather than human judgment alone [Q2]. The dataset's majority baseline is 38.5% (87 True, 78 False, 61 Unknown in the test set) [Q94], indicating a relatively balanced three-way distribution that prevents trivial majority-class exploitation.

FOL is chosen as the formal substrate because it enables unambiguous derivation of truth values [Q136, Q137], and the benchmark explicitly includes operators for negation, conjunction, disjunction, implication, universal quantification, existential quantification, and equality [Q138], while excluding temporal and modal logics as out of scope [Q139, Q140]. N-place predicates are used where applicable for expressivity [Q141], though Davidsonian semantics are excluded as unnecessary for the predicate arities present [Q142]. Five percent of conclusions have complex syntactic structure that challenges even GPT-4's comprehension [Q120].

For the target deployment, this label ontology is a **critical validity gap** (HIGH priority). The deployment requires natural-language explanations of the conflict mechanism with confidence grading [Q3 elicitation] — bare True/False/Unknown classification labels are explicitly non-actionable for engineers reviewing large numbers of requirements. FOLIO's evaluation apparatus has no mechanism to assess fluency, specificity, or correctness of natural-language rationales; it measures only classification accuracy [Q80]. An "Unknown" label in FOLIO corresponds to a formally undecidable conclusion given the premises [Q66], whereas in the deployment context, "Uncertain" is acceptable only if the system articulates what additional information would resolve the conflict — a capability FOLIO cannot measure at all.

### 5. Annotation Process

FOLIO was annotated by a carefully selected pool of CS undergraduate and graduate students and academic/industry researchers, all of whom are native or near-native English speakers with formal training in first-order logic or semantic parsing [Q16, Q27, Q28, Q31, Q122, Q123]. Annotator selection was treated as a prerequisite for dataset quality: the paper describes detailed criteria for annotator eligibility and multiple training sessions supported by annotation guidelines [Q26, Q30]. NL quality control was handled only by NLP/computational linguistics experts, while FOL quality control was restricted to FOL experts [Q29, Q124, Q125] — a separation of expertise that provided targeted domain review at each stage.

The annotation pipeline involved six distinct stages including WikiLogic collection, HybLogic collection, NL quality control, FOL quality control, NL-FOL alignment, and FOL verification, consuming 980 man-hours in total [Q32]. A significant QA finding was that 39.2% of stories had at least one quality issue and required rewriting [Q48]; FOL formulas were found to suffer from consistency issues in preliminary investigations, necessitating a dedicated additional quality-control round [Q53]. Annotators were given explicit FOL translation protocols to maximize cross-annotator consistency [Q54, Q143, Q144, Q145, Q146, Q147], and commonsense premises that would be implicit in human reasoning were surfaced and added explicitly to the NL and FOL representations [Q55].

For the human baseline, FOLIO's test set was separately annotated by expert (CS students with FOL knowledge) and non-expert (community college / high school students) annotators, both native English speakers [Q107, Q108, Q109, Q110]. Expert annotators achieved 95.98% accuracy versus 61.82% for non-experts, a gap of 34.16%, confirming the task's difficulty scales with FOL expertise [Q111]. From a deployment-validity standpoint, the annotator pool — CS students and researchers skilled in FOL — does not include systems engineers with domain knowledge of aerospace, defense, or ICD conventions. Ground-truth labels therefore reflect what is provable from stated premises using formal logic, not what practicing engineers would recognize as a latent conflict between physical constraints or referenced-but-unstated standards.

### 6. Evaluation Metrics and Output Modality

The primary evaluation metric for the NL reasoning task is **accuracy** — the fraction of conclusions assigned the correct True/False/Unknown label [Q80]. For the NL-FOL translation task, two metrics are reported: **Syntactic Validity (SynV)**, a binary indicator of whether all generated FOL formulas pass syntactic parsing [Q73, Q74], and **Inference Engine Execution Accuracy (ExcAcc)**, which feeds translated FOL through the theorem prover and checks whether the resulting truth-value outputs match gold labels [Q75, Q76]. The paper acknowledges that these translation metrics are imperfect and flags the need for a more reliable NL-FOL translation metric as future work [Q77].

The inference engine used is a compact module from Stanford's CS221 course, which required the authors to develop a custom FOL parser/converter in Python to bridge between the human-readable FOL syntax (Russell and Norvig) and the engine's input format [Q148, Q149, Q150, Q151, Q153]. Model evaluation spans fine-tuning (with five random training-set samples averaged for stability [Q93]) and few-shot prompting up to 8 shots [Q95]. Readability of the dataset is quantified using the Dale-Chall Readability Formula [Q59].

Key findings relevant to validity include: GPT-4 performs substantially below expert-human accuracy (a gap of 31.82%) [Q113]; models systematically overpredict "True" relative to "False" and "Unknown" [Q155, Q157], with False/Unknown accuracy at only 54–62% [Q156]; performance degrades sharply with reasoning depth, especially beyond 3 hops [Q99]; and GPT-4 uses commonsense shortcuts leading to incorrect labels in 5% of analyzed errors [Q121]. Premise ordering was confirmed to carry no spurious correlation signal (accuracy changes ~1% under shuffling) [Q158, Q159, Q160]. NL-FOL syntactic validity is ~93% for GPT-3.5 and GPT-4, but execution accuracy is low, indicating models produce syntactically well-formed but semantically incorrect FOL [Q96, Q97].

For the deployment context, none of these metrics assess the quality of natural-language explanations of conflict mechanisms, confidence calibration, or cross-document traceability — the three properties the deployment explicitly requires [Q3 elicitation, Q4 elicitation]. Classification accuracy on FOLIO is therefore only weakly predictive of deployment performance; the evaluation modality is fundamentally mismatched.

### 7. Stated Limitations

The authors are explicit that existing NL reasoning benchmarks — including FOLIO's predecessors — are inadequate for measuring complex logical reasoning [Q8, Q9, Q10], with specific criticisms directed at RuleTaker and LogicNLI for shallow reasoning depth [Q11], limited AST variety [Q12], and tiny vocabularies [Q13], as well as their lack of human-written, real-world grounding [Q14]. FOLIO is positioned as addressing these gaps, but the authors themselves acknowledge several remaining limitations.

On dataset scale, the authors note that the rigorous expert-annotation process constrained growth: scaling up would have required resources beyond the project's means, preventing investigation of how training-data volume affects fine-tuning performance [Q114, Q115, Q116]. They explicitly invite the community to extend the annotation protocol [Q117]. On output measurement, the paper acknowledges that NL-FOL translation metrics are insufficiently reliable and defers a better metric to future work [Q77]. On LLM capability, the authors observe that GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 struggle with HybLogic's higher logical complexity [Q103] and that LLMs show limited ability to extrapolate to long reasoning chains [Q101].

Several scoping limitations are directly relevant to the deployment context. First, FOLIO deliberately excludes temporal and modal logics [Q139, Q140] — both potentially relevant to timing requirements and conditional system states in engineering specifications. Second, Davidsonian semantics are excluded as unnecessary for the predicate arities present [Q142], which may constrain expressivity for event-centric or action-based specification language. Third, the dataset steers clear of identity-based biases [Q47] but accepts "psychologically fundamental generalizations" that may not be factually invariant [Q128], a scoping choice that prioritizes logical and semantic nuance over strict factual accuracy. The authors do not discuss limitations related to implicit premises, cross-document reasoning, domain-specific vocabulary, or the gap between classification and explanation generation — all of which are core to the deployment context and represent undocumented validity risks.
