### 1. Task Taxonomy / Test Case Categories

TOXIGEN is fundamentally structured around two generative tasks: producing toxic and benign statements that implicitly reference minority identity groups, achieved through demonstration-based prompting [Q29, Q36] and an adversarial classifier-in-the-loop decoding algorithm called ALICE [Q14, Q15]. The taxonomy distinguishes between "false negative" examples (toxic prompts steered toward benign classifier outputs) and "false positive" examples (benign prompts steered toward toxic classifier outputs), explicitly targeting the failure modes of existing toxicity detectors [Q46, Q47]. A core design goal is controlling for implicit rather than explicit toxicity — statements that lack profanity or slurs but remain harmful — which the authors treat as a distinct, underserved detection category [Q13, Q32]. The dataset is balanced across 13 identity groups to limit spurious identity-toxicity correlations, explicitly acknowledging that groups such as Native Americans are often overlooked in existing corpora [Q12]; the ALICE-generated subset is further intended to produce adversarially hard examples that fool classifiers [Q50, Q84, Q94].

For the Gazipur deployment context, this taxonomy is deeply misaligned. All 13 identity groups are defined within a U.S. social context (e.g., African Americans, Jewish Americans, LGBTQ+ folks [Q3]), and the "implicit toxicity" framing — while conceptually relevant — was operationalized exclusively around Western cultural harm patterns. The fault lines of relevance in Bangladeshi clinical chatbot interactions (religious minority targeting via terms like "malaun," caste-coded insults, son-preference framing, colorism-based health requests, superstition-embedded harm) are structurally absent from this taxonomy. There is no subtask category for health-norm-embedded toxicity or for religious-minority targeting in a Muslim-majority South Asian context, representing a near-total ontological mismatch on the IO dimension.

### 2. Data Sources and Collection

TOXIGEN is an entirely machine-generated dataset of 274,186 statements produced by GPT-3 [Q1, Q2], making it the largest dataset of its kind and methodologically distinct from crowdsourced or scraped corpora. The generation process required human-crafted seed demonstrations: benign demonstrations were collected from blog posts and news articles [Q37], while toxic demonstrations were sourced from hate forums and Reddit [Q42]. Through a human-in-the-loop process, 20–50 demonstration sentences per group were iteratively refined and expanded [Q41, Q44], yielding 26 prompt sets covering 13 groups [Q44]. All 26,000 generated prompts are released alongside the dataset [Q126], and an additional 8,960 training examples were randomly sampled for large-scale human annotation [Q110, Q111, Q112].

From the perspective of a Bangladeshi clinical deployment, the sourcing of seed demonstrations from U.S.-centric hate forums and English-language news articles [Q37, Q42] means that the cultural knowledge encoded into GPT-3's generations reflects a Western implicit toxicity distribution. GPT-3 itself was trained predominantly on English-language web text, and its known tendency to conflate identities [Q80] would be especially pronounced for South Asian minority groups entirely absent from its prompting regime. There is no data sourced from Bangladeshi social media (e.g., Facebook, which dominates in Bangladesh), no Bengali-language content, and no community-contributed examples from the target population — a foundational IC mismatch.

### 3. Data Format and Preprocessing

TOXIGEN data consists of fluent, machine-generated English text with implicit toxicity — by design devoid of profanity or slurs [Q27] and generated to be nearly indistinguishable from human-written language [Q7, Q20]. The input format is plain text, with generation length varying across the dataset [Q49] and a maximum generation length of 30 tokens imposed during ALICE decoding [Q44]. The dataset is released as a structured dataframe with fields for prompt, generation text, generation method (ALICE vs. top-k), prompt label (binary toxic/benign), target group, and a RoBERTa model prediction score [Q122]. A test set of 792 statements was held out with cosine similarity filtering (threshold 0.7) to prevent train-test overlap [Q63], and the released data is balanced in terms of toxic and benign samples per group [Q102].

This data format presents a severe IF mismatch for the target deployment. The Gazipur deployment receives inputs in degraded Banglish (Bengali in Roman/Latin script), phonetic ASCII Bengali, code-switched text with irregular punctuation and non-standard spacing — none of which resembles the clean, fluent, well-formed English sentences in TOXIGEN [Q4, Q121]. Prompt engineering for TOXIGEN explicitly targeted group-mentioning English text [Q27, Q120], and the dataset contains no multilingual, transliterated, or orthographically degraded examples. Classifiers fine-tuned on TOXIGEN's fluent English generations are highly unlikely to generalize to the noisy, code-switched signal distribution of this deployment — a concern not addressed anywhere in the paper.

### 4. Label Categories and Output Types

TOXIGEN's primary label is a binary toxic/benign distinction derived from prompt labels, but the human evaluation introduces a more nuanced 1–5 harmfulness scale applied across two conditions: harm if written by AI (HARMFULIFAI) and harm if written by a human (HARMFULIFHUMAN), reflecting the authors' hypothesis that readers apply different standards to machine-generated content [Q57, Q58]. The maximum of these two scores is then mapped into three classes: non-toxic (< 3), ambiguous (= 3), and toxic (> 3) [Q67]. Additional label dimensions captured during annotation include perceived harmful intent (HARMFULINTENT) [Q59], positive stereotyping (POSSTEREO) [Q60], lewdness (LEWD), group framing (WHICHGROUP, GROUPFRAMING), and fact-vs-opinion classification (FACTOROPINION) [Q62]. Notably, 94.56% of toxic examples are further labeled as hate speech [Q73], and "moral judgement" — questioning a group's morality — is identified as the most common framing tactic [Q83].

For the Bangladeshi deployment context, this label ontology reflects a U.S.-derived harm model. The three-class output (non-toxic / ambiguous / toxic) does not distinguish between the locally relevant harm categories of gendered health-norm toxicity, religious minority insults, colorism, and superstition-based harm — all of which would require distinct decision boundaries calibrated to local values. The binary fact/opinion label [Q62, Q74] is particularly insufficient for a clinical chatbot context where harmful superstition-based health advice may be presented as factual and require moderation on different grounds than identity-based hate speech. This represents a substantial OO mismatch.

### 5. Annotation Process

Human annotation in TOXIGEN was conducted on a sample of 792 test statements, each rated by 3 annotators drawn from a pool of 156 prequalified workers on Amazon Mechanical Turk (MTurk) [Q64], with a larger-scale validation of 8,960 training examples using the same pool [Q110]. The annotation framework was multi-dimensional, asking annotators to judge human-vs-AI authorship, harmfulness under two authorship conditions, harmful intent, positive stereotyping, lewdness, group framing, and factuality [Q55, Q56, Q57, Q59, Q60, Q61]. Inter-annotator agreement was moderate, with Fleiss' κ = 0.46 and Krippendorff's α = 0.64 [Q68], and majority agreement (≥ 2/3 annotators) was achieved in 93.4% of cases [Q69]. The authors acknowledge that annotators' sociodemographic backgrounds and belief systems affect toxicity perception [Q118], and a specific failure case is documented where annotators did not recognize MGTOW as a misogynistic group [Q119].

This annotation setup has critical OC validity limitations for the Gazipur deployment. The MTurk annotator pool [Q64] is predominantly composed of U.S.-based workers and is not described as including any South Asian, Bengali-speaking, or in-region Bangladeshi annotators. The annotation questions were designed for U.S. cultural harm concepts, and there is no evidence that annotators had the contextual knowledge required to recognize locally specific toxicity patterns such as caste-coded slurs, religious minority targeting in a Muslim-majority context, or harmful Bangladeshi health beliefs. The documented failure case [Q119] — where annotators missed a misogynistic group identifier they did not recognize — is directly analogous to the risk of U.S. annotators missing toxicity encoded in culturally specific Bangladeshi terms. Prior work cited by the authors [Q118] explicitly warns of this annotator background effect, but no corrective action was taken to include diverse regional annotators. A RoBERTa classifier fine-tuned on these annotations [Q123] and released as part of the dataset propagates these cultural blind spots into any downstream system that relies on it [Q124].

### 6. Evaluation Metrics and Output Modality

TOXIGEN's primary downstream evaluation measures whether fine-tuning toxicity classifiers on TOXIGEN improves performance on existing human-written implicit hate speech datasets — specifically ImplicitHateCorpus, SocialBiasFrames, and DynaHate — with improvements of +7–19% reported [Q23, Q96, Q98]. Robustness of the ALICE-generated subset is evaluated by measuring how often ALICE-decoded sentences fool HateBERT [Q89, Q114], and detoxification effectiveness is measured by comparing average human-annotated toxicity scores between ALICE and top-k decoding conditions [Q90, Q91]. The human-like quality of machine-generated text is assessed by measuring annotator misclassification rate (90.5% of examples thought to be human-written) [Q71, Q104]. Inter-annotator agreement [Q65, Q66, Q68] and distribution of toxicity scores across generation method and identity group [Q127, Q128] are also reported.

All evaluation benchmarks used as external validation targets (ImplicitHateCorpus, SocialBiasFrames, DynaHate) [Q96] are English-language, U.S.-context datasets. No evaluation is conducted on Bengali-language, Banglish, or South Asian hate speech data, and no robustness metrics are reported for noisy or code-switched input conditions. The metric of "fooling HateBERT" [Q89, Q103] evaluates classifier robustness against adversarial English text — a meaningfully different adversarial threat model than the unintentional degraded-text toxicity that would characterize low-literacy user inputs in Gazipur. Fine-tuning improvements demonstrated on clean English datasets [Q99, Q100] provide no evidence of transfer to the target deployment's signal distribution, and the OF dimension — text-based scoring output — is the only area of partial alignment.

### 7. Stated Limitations

The authors acknowledge several relevant limitations. Implicit toxicity remains an "elusive goal" for NLP systems [Q24], is not marked by profanity, can be positive in sentiment, and is hard to collect at scale [Q25] — challenges that are compounded rather than resolved in a low-resource Bangladeshi language context. The dataset explicitly excludes identity dimensions associated with "agents of oppression" (e.g., whiteness, heterosexuality) [Q106], reflecting a design choice rooted in U.S. power dynamics that does not transfer to the Bangladeshi context where majority-minority power relations are structured very differently. The authors note that toxicity is "inherently subjective" [Q108] and that annotations "might not capture the full complexity of these issues related to human experiences" [Q107] — a particularly salient caveat given that none of the annotators share the cultural context of the deployment population.

Technical limitations include API-imposed vocabulary restrictions during ALICE decoding [Q45] and the acknowledged difficulty of prompt engineering [Q34], as well as the challenge of finding large amounts of implicit toxic data at scale [Q38]. GPT-3's tendency to conflate identities or mention multiple groups when generating for a specific target group [Q80] introduces label noise that the authors acknowledge but do not fully resolve. Annotation subjectivity is flagged as an ongoing concern [Q117], and a concrete failure case — where annotators failed to recognize MGTOW as harmful [Q119] — illustrates the cultural knowledge dependency of the annotation framework. Crucially, the authors do not state any limitation regarding language coverage, script representation, or geographic scope of the benchmark, meaning these are gaps that must be inferred rather than acknowledged — particularly concerning for a deployment in a non-English, non-Western context.

### 8. Authors and Affiliations

The authors are Thomas Hartvigsen, Saadia Gabriel, Hamid Palangi, Maarten Sap, Dipankar Ray, and Ece Kamar [Q10], affiliated with MIT, University of Washington, Microsoft Research, the Allen Institute for AI, Carnegie Mellon University, and Microsoft [Q11]. Compute and large-scale human study support was provided by Azure AI Platform and Microsoft Research [Q109]. This is an entirely U.S.-based, English-language NLP research team from elite Western institutions; there is no indication of any author with South Asian NLP expertise, Bengali-language background, or familiarity with the Bangladeshi clinical or cultural context. The institutional affiliations signal that the benchmark was designed, validated, and scoped entirely within a U.S. research context, which is consistent with and explanatory of the ontological, content, and annotator mismatches identified across all other dimensions relative to the Gazipur deployment scenario.
