### 1. Task Taxonomy / Test Case Categories

TOXIGEN is fundamentally a binary toxic/benign classification task structured around 13 U.S.-defined minority identity groups, with the stated goal of generating equal numbers of toxic and benign statements per group [Q12]. A key design priority is the generation of *implicit* toxicity — statements devoid of profanity or slurs that nonetheless cause harm — because such content is by definition underrepresented in existing corpora [Q13]. The benchmark introduces two generation regimes: standard demonstration-based prompting (top-k decoding) and ALICE, an adversarial classifier-in-the-loop algorithm designed to produce statements that fool toxicity classifiers [Q14, Q15]. ALICE operates in two modes: steering a language model toward false negatives (toxic content that evades classifiers) and toward false positives (benign content that triggers classifiers) [Q46, Q47], with the ALICE-generated subset explicitly positioned as a challenging adversarial evaluation layer [Q50, Q84]. A downstream validation task is also defined, in which fine-tuning classifiers on TOXIGEN is assessed for generalization to human-written implicit hate datasets [Q94]. From the perspective of the South Asian deployment context, the taxonomy is critically deficient: the 13 groups (e.g., African Americans, women, LGBTQ+ individuals) [Q3] are exclusively U.S.-framed, with no representation of caste-based groups (Dalits, OBCs), Hindu–Muslim communal conflict categories, India–Pakistan political rhetoric, or party-specific slur targets — categories that are central to the deployment and have no meaningful equivalent in TOXIGEN's ontology [Q32].

### 2. Data Sources and Collection

TOXIGEN's 274,186 statements are entirely machine-generated using GPT-3, selected because of its capacity to produce human-like text while also being prone to generating socially biased content [Q1, Q2]. The dataset is balanced, containing over 135,000 toxic and 135,000 benign statements across the 13 target groups [Q3, Q31]. Seed demonstrations for toxic prompts were sourced from hate forums and Reddit [Q42], while benign demonstrations were drawn from blog posts and news articles mentioning each group [Q37] — a 20–50 sentence seed set per group, all released with the dataset [Q41]. A human-in-the-loop process was used iteratively to expand and refine demonstration sets for both toxic and benign categories [Q39, Q40, Q43], with all 26,000 prompts released publicly [Q126]. Two generation variants are included: top-k sampled and ALICE-generated, drawn in roughly equal proportions in the large-scale annotated training sample [Q111, Q112]. For the South Asian deployment, the data source provenance is a significant validity concern: seed content originates from U.S.-centric hate forums and English-language news, and no South Asian linguistic or cultural registers — including code-mixed Hinglish, Romanized Urdu, or Tanglish — are represented. The machine-generation process [Q101] amplifies rather than corrects this gap, as GPT-3's training distribution itself skews heavily toward Western English content.

### 3. Data Format and Preprocessing

All TOXIGEN data are text-only, generated via demonstration-based prompting in which example sentences are passed to GPT-3 to elicit similar but distinct outputs [Q4, Q27, Q28]. Generation parameters include a maximum length of 30 tokens, beam size of 10, and temperature of 0.9 [Q44], with generation length varying across the final dataset [Q49]. The data are released as a dataframe with fields for prompt text, generated text, generation method (ALICE vs. top-k), binary prompt label, target group, and a RoBERTa-model toxicity probability score [Q122]. Test set construction enforced a cosine similarity threshold of ≤0.7 between training and test items [Q63], and the released data are balanced in terms of toxic/benign counts per group [Q102]. The prompt engineering approach uses demonstration injection to encourage group-mentioning, implicit text [Q120, Q121], and the quality of generated text is reported to reflect diverse group-specific references [Q21]. For the South Asian deployment context, the text-only modality aligns with the deployment's needs [Q51], but the English-only, standard-orthography format presents a moderate mismatch: code-mixed Romanized scripts (Hinglish, Romanized Urdu, Tanglish) differ substantially in vocabulary, spelling conventions, and syntactic structure from the clean English text TOXIGEN produces. Prompt engineering quality is also noted as highly consequential [Q120], suggesting that adapting TOXIGEN's prompting framework to South Asian registers would require substantial new seed curation rather than simple translation.

### 4. Label Categories and Output Types

TOXIGEN's primary label is a binary prompt label (toxic = 1, benign = 0), acknowledged as "slightly noisy" but "largely accurate" by human annotators [Q122]. For the human-validated test set, a richer label schema is employed: harmfulness is rated on a 1–5 Likert scale under two conditions — whether the author was perceived as AI or human (HARMFULIFAI, HARMFULIFHUMAN) — with the maximum of the two mapped into three classes: non-toxic (<3), ambiguous (=3), and toxic (>3) [Q58, Q67]. Additional label dimensions capture: intended harm (HARMFULINTENT) [Q59], positive stereotyping (POSSTEREO) [Q60], lewdness (LEWD), group framing (WHICHGROUP, GROUPFRAMING), and factual vs. opinion framing (FACTOROPINION) [Q62]. Implicit toxicity is the dominant form — 98.2% of statements lack explicit profanity [Q5] — and most toxic examples are categorized as hate speech (94.56%) [Q73], with moral judgment identified as the most common framing tactic [Q83]. For the South Asian deployment, the multi-dimensional Likert structure partially aligns with the requirement for graded, multi-axis ratings [Q58]; however, the label dimensions were designed to capture U.S.-calibrated harm constructs. Dimensions critical to South Asian deployment — caste-based target specificity, communal religious framing, implicit political dog-whistles recognizable only to in-group South Asian readers — are entirely absent from the label ontology. The ambiguity class [Q67] and the POSSTEREO label [Q60] offer partial structural analogues to the deployment's need for implicitness and positive-stereotype capture, but without South Asian cultural grounding these categories cannot substitute for purpose-built regional labels.

### 5. Annotation Process

Human validation was conducted on a 792-statement test set (TOXIGEN-HUMANVAL), with each statement rated by 3 annotators drawn from a pool of 156 pre-qualified Amazon Mechanical Turk workers [Q52, Q64]. Annotators answered a multi-dimensional battery of questions covering perceived authorship (human vs. AI), harmfulness under both authorship conditions, intended harm, positive stereotyping, lewdness, group framing, and factual vs. opinion status [Q55, Q56, Q57, Q59, Q61]. Inter-annotator agreement was moderate (Fleiss' κ = 0.46, Krippendorff's α = 0.64), with majority agreement (≥2/3 annotators) in 93.4% of cases [Q68, Q69] — figures the authors note are comparable to or higher than prior hate speech annotation work [Q66]. A human-in-the-loop process was also used iteratively during data generation to refine demonstration sets [Q39, Q43], and a larger annotated training sample of 8,960 examples was subsequently collected using the same MTurk pool [Q110]. A fine-tuned RoBERTa classifier was trained on this larger annotated set and released with the dataset [Q123]. Critically for the South Asian deployment, annotator demographics are not documented beyond platform (MTurk) and qualification status. Prior work flagged in the paper itself notes that annotators' sociodemographic backgrounds systematically affect toxicity perception [Q118], and the user's deployment context explicitly anticipates systematic disagreement across Indian, Pakistani, and Sri Lankan annotators on identical stimuli. TOXIGEN's annotation pool almost certainly lacks South Asian regional diversity, and the 90% human-likeness finding [Q104, Q20] was validated by annotators with no disclosed expertise in South Asian languages or cultural contexts — meaning the quality assurance framework does not transfer to the target deployment. The risk of annotators failing to recognize in-group toxicity is directly illustrated by Q119, where annotators mislabeled a misogynistic group reference due to lack of cultural knowledge, a pattern that would be systematically amplified across South Asian content.

### 6. Evaluation Metrics and Output Modality

TOXIGEN is evaluated across several complementary metrics. At the classifier-robustness level, ALICE-generated statements are assessed by how often they fool five publicly available toxicity classifiers [Q18], with ALICE outperforming top-k sampling on adversarial fooling rate (26.4% vs. 16.8% at small scale [Q89]; 58.97% vs. 26.88% at larger scale [Q114]). Human-annotation quality is assessed via Fleiss' κ and Krippendorff's α [Q68], and the proportion of machine-generated statements mistaken for human-written text is reported at 90.5% overall, rising to 92.9% for toxic examples [Q71, Q72]. Classifier fine-tuning performance is measured on three external human-written datasets (ImplicitHateCorpus, SocialBiasFrames, DynaHate) and on TOXIGEN-HUMANVAL itself, with improvements of +7–19% reported [Q23, Q96, Q98, Q99]. Average toxicity scores on a 1–5 scale are used to compare ALICE vs. top-k outputs [Q78, Q90, Q127], and group-mention accuracy (the proportion of generations referencing the intended target group) is tracked at both small and large scale [Q79, Q116, Q128]. The output modality is a graded scalar score plus group-mention classification, with downstream outputs being binary or three-class toxicity decisions [Q67, Q95]. For the South Asian deployment — which requires comparative ranking between South Asian and Western-equivalent stimuli and multi-dimensional ratings beyond a single severity axis — TOXIGEN's metric framework is partially relevant (Likert-scale grading [Q58, Q78] is compatible) but insufficient: it does not decompose scores by implicitness level, target-group specificity, or cross-regional comparability, and all evaluation benchmarks [Q96] are U.S.-framed English datasets with no South Asian validation data.

### 7. Stated Limitations

The authors explicitly acknowledge that implicit toxicity detection remains an unsolved problem in NLP [Q24], and that implicit toxicity is inherently harder to collect and detect because it lacks surface markers such as profanity [Q25], yet causes documented psychological harm to targeted communities [Q26]. Prompt engineering is noted as notoriously difficult [Q34], and finding large quantities of implicit toxicity data at scale is acknowledged as a core challenge motivating the machine-generation approach [Q38]. A vocabulary restriction imposed by the OpenAI API limited ALICE's constrained decoding to two tokens per group [Q45], and GPT-3 is noted to sometimes conflate identities or mention multiple groups in a single generation [Q80]. The authors flag that toxicity is inherently subjective [Q108] and that annotations may not capture the full complexity of human experiences with toxic language [Q107]. The benchmark explicitly excludes identity dimensions associated with historically dominant groups (whiteness, heterosexuality, able-bodied-ness), framing this as a normative choice to center targets of oppression [Q106]. Annotator subjectivity is flagged as a concern, including the documented risk that annotators may fail to recognize toxicity directed at groups they are unfamiliar with [Q117, Q119]. For the South Asian deployment, these acknowledged limitations compound severely: the subjectivity caveat [Q108] and annotator-knowledge gap [Q119] directly anticipate the cross-national disagreement the user expects across Indian, Pakistani, and Sri Lankan raters. The exclusion of dominant-group identity dimensions [Q106] has no principled mapping to South Asian power dynamics, where the same community may be a target in one national context and a dominant group in another. The broader admission that the dataset does not capture the full complexity of toxic language [Q107] is especially salient when the deployment requires code-mixed, regionally specific, and historically embedded language varieties entirely outside TOXIGEN's generative scope. The ALICE vocabulary restriction [Q45] would similarly constrain any adaptation attempt using the same API-based framework.

The institutional profile is uniformly U.S.-based, spanning elite U.S. academic and industry research institutions, with no South Asian institutional affiliations or community-based partnerships evident in the registry. This origin is validity-relevant: the benchmark's target-group taxonomy, annotation pool, seed data sources, and evaluation datasets all reflect a U.S. research context, and no author affiliation signals expertise in South Asian languages, caste dynamics, or regional communal conflict — the core domains of the target deployment.
