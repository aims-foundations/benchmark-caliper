### 1. Task Taxonomy / Test Case Categories

INJONGO targets two canonical conversational AI tasks — intent detection and slot-filling — which together form the natural language understanding module of task-oriented dialogue systems [Q1, Q2]. These tasks map user utterances to predefined semantic categories and extract typed slot entities [Q3], collectively constituting a joint intent detection and slot-filling (ID-SF) dataset [Q31]. The benchmark spans 40 intents organized across 5 domains (Banking, Home, Travel, Kitchen & Dining, Utility) [Q117], with the travel domain being the most directly relevant to the Ethiopian intercity booking deployment context. The task taxonomy also encompasses named entity extraction as a component of slot-filling, framed explicitly as identifying specific slot types from utterances [Q147, Q148, Q150, Q151], with close kinship to NER benchmarks such as MasakhaNER [Q25] but at finer granularity across multiple domains. For evaluation, the paper tests encoder-only, encoder-decoder, and LLM2Vec transformer architectures [Q61], as well as zero-shot and few-shot LLM prompting strategies [Q74], and cross-lingual transfer from English to African languages — including a comparison between Western-centric (CLINC) and African-centric (INJONGO) English source data [Q101]. A supervised fine-tuning (SFT) baseline on Gemma 2 9B is also provided as a strong upper-bound comparison [Q71], and results are presented as the final summarizing benchmark [Q105].

From a deployment validity perspective, the travel domain's 40 intents were selected from CLINC as "most suitable to African contexts" [Q39], but the specific subset of travel intents — covering items such as "exchange rate" and "book flight" — does not appear to include operator-specific intercity bus flows (seat-class selection, road-closure rebooking, or holiday-surge booking). This is an input-ontology gap worth flagging for the Sky Bus / Selam Bus / Ride deployment context.

### 2. Data Sources and Collection

INJONGO is the first large-scale multicultural ID-SF dataset covering 16 African languages plus English, described as the largest of its kind for African Conversational AI [Q4, Q106]. The 16 languages were chosen to represent typological diversity, drawing from two dominant African language families: 13 Niger-Congo languages and 3 Afro-Asiatic languages, with speaker populations ranging from 98 million (Swahili) to 5 million (Wolof) [Q32, Q33, Q34]. The source intent taxonomy was derived from the English CLINC dataset (150 intents, 10 domains), from which 40 intents across 5 domains most relevant to African contexts were selected [Q39]. Rather than translating CLINC utterances directly — an approach criticized for translationese artifacts and Western cultural bias [Q13, Q27] — annotators were provided CLINC sample sentences as prompts and asked to independently generate culturally relevant utterances in their native language [Q37]. The final dataset contains 3,200 annotated utterances per African language (80 per intent) [Q18, Q56], with an English set of 1,779 utterances assembled from annotator practice outputs [Q58, Q59], and an additional 4,000 CLINC samples retained for Western-centric vs. African-centric comparison experiments [Q60]. A machine translation model (NLLB-200-3.3B) was employed for the Translate-Test cross-lingual transfer setting [Q75]. Dataset statistics including token counts and inter-annotator agreement measures are documented in a summary table [Q120], and the dataset is released under CC BY 4.0 [Q23].

For the Ethiopian intercity booking deployment, a critical data-source concern is the slot entity content within the travel domain. Annotators were encouraged to replace Western entities with locally relevant ones [Q41], and Swahili's relatively high web-data coverage appears to benefit model performance [Q88, Q89], suggesting that data distribution varies substantially across languages. Amharic is an Afro-Asiatic language included in INJONGO, but whether its travel-domain slot instances contain Ethiopian-specific toponyms (Hawassa, Mekelle, Gondar), Birr-denominated fares, or Ethiopian calendar date references is not confirmed by any quote in this registry — a significant IC-dimension gap.

### 3. Data Format and Preprocessing

Data construction followed two stages: utterance elicitation in the target African language, followed by slot-filling annotation of the generated utterance [Q38]. Annotation was performed on the LabelStudio platform using a named entity recognition setup [Q43], with the output format representing slot spans as labeled entity types. An example output format uses `$$` as a delimiter between entity type and value, and between entities [Q146], which is also the format used in LLM prompting [Q145]. Before finalization, an entity frequency analysis was conducted across all languages [Q50]; slot types appearing fewer than 500 times across all languages were removed [Q51, Q52], and similar or low-frequency types were merged, reducing the original 34 slot types to 23 final slot types [Q55, Q119]. The dataset is partitioned into 70/10/20 train/dev/test splits, stratified by intent [Q57]. For model inference, closed-source models (GPT-4o, Gemini 1.5 Pro) were accessed via vendor APIs [Q131], while open-source models used vLLM or TGI frameworks [Q132]; all fine-tuning experiments ran in FP32 on NVIDIA H100/L40S GPUs with batch size 32 [Q129]. Few-shot shot-count conventions differ by task: for slot-filling, shots are defined per slot type (e.g., 1-shot = 1 example per slot type = 23 examples total) [Q143]. For the Ethiopian deployment context, the text-only, script-level format of INJONGO fully matches the smartphone text interface, and Amharic script is covered — making input form (IF) a low-concern dimension. One flagged preprocessing gap is whether the DATE slot type accommodates Ethiopian (Ge'ez) calendar months such as Meskerem or Tikimt, since the annotation guide examples use Gregorian conventions [Q153, Q154].

### 4. Label Categories and Output Types

The output label space consists of two components: (1) an intent label drawn from 40 categories across 5 domains [Q117], and (2) slot entity labels drawn from 23 final slot types [Q118, Q119]. The 23 final slot types are explicitly enumerated in the benchmark's prompts [Q144]: ACCOUNT_TYPE, ARTIST_NAME, BANK_NAME, BILL_TYPE, CALENDAR_EVENT, CITY_OR_PROVINCE, COUNTRY, CURRENCY, DATE, DISH_OR_FOOD, HOTEL_NAME, LANGUAGE_NAME, MEAL_PERIOD, MONEY, MUSIC_GENRE, NUMBER, PAYMENT_COMPANY, PERSONAL_NAME, PLACE_NAME, RESTAURANT_NAME, SHOPPING_ITEM, SONG_NAME, TIME. The annotation guide provides fine-grained label boundary rules — for example, TIME applies to sub-day durations and DATE to periods of a day or more [Q153, Q154], BANK_NAME should exclude the word "bank" unless it is part of the name [Q152], and BILL_TYPE annotations include the word "bill" [Q155]. The full original 34-type ontology is documented in Appendix A.2 [Q54]. For the Ethiopian intercity booking deployment, the CURRENCY and MONEY slot types are present — supporting Birr-denominated fare extraction in principle — but whether the training instances contain Birr (ETB) values versus other African currencies is unknown from the registry alone. Critically, the DATE slot is Gregorian-centric by convention (the annotation guide examples use Gregorian dates), and there is no documented accommodation for Ethiopian calendar months, which is a direct OC-dimension risk for travel-date slot extraction in the target deployment.

### 5. Annotation Process

Utterances were generated and annotated by native speakers of each target language [Q7], recruited through a logistics company based in Kenya that handled compensation at per-country rates [Q44]. The Xhosa annotator example illustrates the elicitation methodology: annotators were shown a CLINC sentence with a specified intent and asked to produce a culturally grounded equivalent — for instance, substituting South African banks (FNB, Absa) and the Rand currency [Q40, Q41]. Each utterance received slot-filling annotations from at least three annotators, with disagreements resolved by majority voting [Q42, Q45]. Initial Fleiss' Kappa scores showed substantial cross-language variation (0.618 for Zulu to 0.934 for Shona) [Q47]; after a review round, scores improved to 0.912–1.00 across all languages [Q48], with the largest gains in Sesotho (+0.327) and Zulu (+0.294) [Q49]. The review process was motivated partly by ambiguous slot type definitions that degraded early annotation quality [Q53], and the annotation guide was detailed enough to include edge-case rules [Q156, Q157] and a complete slot-type instruction set [Q149]. The annotation quality metric used was Fleiss' Kappa [Q113].

From the HIGH-priority OC dimension perspective for the Ethiopian deployment: while native Amharic speakers annotated the Amharic portion, the specific regional or demographic profile of those annotators — urban vs. rural origin, Addis Ababa–centric vs. other regions — is not disclosed anywhere in this registry. This is a material validity gap, because the actual user population includes L2 Amharic speakers from Oromia, Tigray, and Amhara regions who may code-switch. INJONGO includes Oromo and Tigrinya as separate languages [Q4] but does not document intra-utterance code-switching within Amharic utterances, and annotator diversity sub-regional to the Amharic context is simply not addressed. The two-round review process [Q109] improves internal consistency but cannot compensate for systematic demographic underrepresentation if the annotator pool skews toward Addis Ababa.

### 6. Evaluation Metrics and Output Modality

The benchmark reports intent detection performance using accuracy and slot-filling performance using F1-score [Q84]. The best fine-tuned model (AfroXLMR-76L) achieves 93.7% accuracy on intent detection and 85.6% F1 on slot-filling [Q19, Q78]; GPT-4o in zero-shot achieves 70.6% average intent detection accuracy [Q10] and only 26% F1 on slot-filling [Q9], with the gap between fine-tuning and best prompting being −28 accuracy points and −52.6 F1 points [Q20]. Few-shot prompting substantially closes the slot-filling gap — GPT-4o and Gemini 1.5 Pro each improve by more than +19 F1 points with just 5 examples [Q91] — and SFT on Gemma 2 9B remains 34.1 F1 points above 4-shot prompting [Q98]. Cross-lingual transfer experiments show that using African-centric English (INJONGO) versus Western-centric English (CLINC) yields a +16.1 accuracy gain for in-language transfer and +29 accuracy gain in translate-test at 10-shots [Q102, Q103], though this advantage disappears at 25-shots [Q104]. All fine-tuning runs use five random seeds [Q63, Q76], AdamW with linear warmup/decay for 20 epochs with early stopping [Q122, Q123], and learning rates calibrated per architecture and task [Q124, Q125]. LLM evaluations use 5 prompt templates and temperature 0.5 [Q73], with best-prompt selection (2nd prompt for ID, 3rd for SF) applied for few-shot comparisons [Q134, Q135], and the final evaluation uses accuracy/F1 averaged over templates and African languages [Q84]. Prompts are language-specific and designed to guide structured entity extraction [Q137, Q138]. For the Ethiopian deployment context, F1-score as the slot-filling metric is well-suited to partial-match assessment of slot spans, and accuracy for intent detection is appropriate given the 40-class closed label space — the output modality (OF) is a strong match to the deployment's structured intent+slot output requirements.

### 7. Stated Limitations

The authors directly acknowledge several limitation categories that are highly relevant to the Ethiopian deployment context. First, regarding linguistic scope: current benchmarks (including predecessors) predominantly reflect Western-centric concepts due to translation from English [Q12], and even MASSIVE — a widely used multilingual benchmark — includes only three African languages, many of whose utterances "remain culturally irrelevant to the target language communities" [Q14]. INJONGO itself acknowledges that its 16-language coverage "still represents only a fraction of Africa's linguistic diversity, particularly lacking representation from other language families such as Nilo-Saharan languages" [Q108], and the dataset size of 3,200 examples per language "remains modest compared to high-resource benchmarks" [Q110].

Second, regarding domain and intent coverage: the benchmark covers only 5 domains and 40 intents, "missing some other domains such as healthcare and education that are essential for real-world applications" [Q107]. For the intercity booking deployment, the exclusion of domains like operator-specific cancellation or seat-class management is an input-ontology gap not acknowledged but implied by this constraint. The fixed distribution of 80 utterances per intent also "may not accurately reflect the natural frequency of these interactions in real-world conversations" [Q111], which could distort model calibration for high-frequency booking intents.

Third, on model performance gaps: LLM performance on slot-filling "is still behind for many low-resource African languages" [Q15, Q22], and open models are "more biased toward high-resource languages" [Q87], with African-language intent detection for open models below 50% in zero-shot [Q86]. Pre-training coverage gaps matter: AfroXLMR was not pre-trained on Ewe, Twi, Lingala, and Wolof, causing significant performance drops [Q81], underscoring that model pre-training coverage of Amharic specifically should be verified. The translationese effect [Q13] and cost of large-scale crowdsourcing for low-resource languages [Q28, Q36] are acknowledged as systemic constraints. The benchmark also notes that ambiguous slot types degraded annotation quality during review [Q53], that MASSIVE's partial cultural replacement still left Western entities prevalent [Q29], and that few-shot LLM improvements, while real, still trail SFT by a large margin [Q97, Q100].
