### 1. Task Taxonomy / Test Case Categories

MRBench introduces a unified evaluation taxonomy comprising eight pedagogical dimensions grounded in learning sciences principles, specifically designed to assess AI tutor responses in the context of student mistake remediation in mathematics [Q1, Q15]. The task is formally defined as a dialogue response generation problem where the tutor must produce the most appropriate next response given a conversation history in which the student's most recent utterances contain a mistake or confusion [Q12, Q13], and the eight dimensions are applied to evaluate that response [Q14]. The eight dimensions — derived through iterative analysis [Q129] — include: encouraging active learning (e.g., withholding the answer and scaffolding guidance) [Q17]; adapting to students' goals (e.g., identifying and locating the mistake) [Q18]; managing cognitive load and metacognitive skills (e.g., structured, chunked guidance) [Q19]; fostering motivation and curiosity [Q20]; mistake identification [Q21, Q22]; not revealing the answer [Q23]; providing actionable guidance [Q24, Q25]; logical coherence [Q26]; appropriate tone (avoiding toxicity) [Q27]; and human-likeness [Q29]. The taxonomy explicitly rejects dimensions like grammaticality and empathy that were initially considered but found unnecessary via pilot validation [Q131, Q132], and is intended to be treated as a set of orthogonal, independent dimensions [Q30, Q86]. The benchmark covers seven state-of-the-art LLM-based tutors and human tutors (novice and expert), assessing their comparative pedagogical abilities [Q53, Q103].

From the perspective of the Indian metropolitan deployment context, this taxonomy presents a significant ontological mismatch on the **input** side: the task categories and pedagogical dimensions are derived from Western learning science principles and validated by annotators without documented ties to Indian classroom pedagogy [Q31]. The framework does not include dimensions measuring cultural appropriateness, register alignment with Indian classroom norms, or curriculum-specific correctness (e.g., NCERT-aligned solution methods). While the taxonomy is comprehensive for generic English-medium math tutoring [Q111, Q112], it does not capture whether a response would be satisfying to a Grade 1–8 student in metropolitan India whose expectations of tutor warmth, formality, and contextual relevance may differ meaningfully from the implicit Western baseline assumed by the benchmark's design [Q144, Q143].

### 2. Data Sources and Collection

MRBench is compiled from two existing educational dialogue datasets: the Bridge dataset [Q44] and the MathDial dataset [Q48], together comprising 192 selected conversation instances [Q2, Q52]. Bridge contains partial dialogue interactions between real human tutors and students at the elementary level, focusing on fundamental math operations such as multiplication and addition [Q44, Q45]; 60 high-quality instances were filtered from its original 700 dialogues, with the primary selection criterion being that the student's last utterance exhibits an error or confusion [Q46, Q47]. MathDial contains complete multi-turn conversations between a real human tutor and an LLM acting as a student, grounded in middle school-level mathematical reasoning [Q48, Q49]; 132 instances were retained after manual quality inspection [Q51]. Seven state-of-the-art LLMs (GPT-4, Gemini, Sonnet, Mistral, Llama-3.1-8B, Llama-3.1-405B, and Phi3) generated tutor responses for all 192 instances, yielding 1,596 total responses [Q52, Q53].

For the Indian deployment context, neither Bridge nor MathDial is documented as containing NCERT-aligned content, Indian numeral conventions (lakhs, crores), rupee-based word problems, or Indian everyday contextual framings. The Bridge dataset is explicitly described as grounded in elementary math operations in short, Western-style dialogues [Q45, Q92], and MathDial is grounded in middle school reasoning problems without documented Indian cultural context [Q95]. The paper itself notes that the Bridge dataset's short, basic problem contexts cause difficulty for LLMs in providing guidance without revealing answers [Q93], a finding that is specific to the Western elementary math framing. Combining both datasets is described as creating diversity [Q97], but this diversity does not extend to cultural or national curriculum variation. The novice responses are drawn solely from Bridge [Q82, Q139], further concentrating any Western framing bias in that stratum. This is a high-priority validity gap: the benchmark's input data instances do not reflect the Indian educational context required by the deployment.

### 3. Data Format and Preprocessing

The benchmark's input format is text-based conversational dialogue in English, with conversation history and tutor/student turns as the primary input signal [Q13, Q133]. To harmonize the two source datasets, Bridge's partial conversations were used directly, while MathDial conversations were truncated at the point of a student mistake to match Bridge's format, with the subsequent human tutor response treated as the expert reference [Q50]. The prompt template used to elicit LLM responses from all seven models was adapted from Wang et al. (2024a) [Q134], and conversation and response lengths are measured in characters [Q147]. Bridge conversations are notably shorter in both history and response length and involve fewer turns than MathDial, as shown in the dataset statistics [Q141, Q142]; MathDial does not provide conversation topic labels [Q145]. The benchmark comprises 192 dialogue instances generating 1,596 total annotated responses across seven LLMs and two human conditions [Q54].

From the deployment perspective, the text-only, English-medium format is well-matched to the target population, which is described as fluent in English and interacting via mobile or enterprise software — no modality or script mismatch is present. This is a LOWER-priority dimension and the benchmark's format is broadly appropriate for the deployment's input conditions.

### 4. Label Categories and Output Types

Each of the eight pedagogical dimensions is annotated using a three-tier labeling system [Q58], exemplified by the "mistake identification" dimension with labels: (i) yes, (ii) to some extent, and (iii) no [Q59]. The desired (gold-standard) label for each dimension is specified in the taxonomy table [Q65, Q128], and these desired labels form the basis for the Desired Annotation Match Rate (DAMR) metric [Q64]. The taxonomy and annotation scheme focus explicitly on the appropriateness of individual tutor turns, not on downstream student learning outcomes [Q119]. When the "Tutor Tone" dimension's labels for neutral and encouraging are combined into "Non-offensive," DAMR reaches 100% across all tutors [Q90], suggesting that the offensive-language boundary is trivially satisfied by current LLMs in this context. Importantly, factual correctness of guidance is a condition for it to be counted as useful in the actionability dimension [Q84], which means a response providing incorrect scaffolding may score lower on guidance even if it prompts student action [Q85].

For the Indian metropolitan deployment, the output label ontology presents a HIGH-priority validity concern: the eight dimensions do not include any label for cultural appropriateness, NCERT curriculum correctness, or register alignment with Indian classroom communication norms. As confirmed in the deployment elicitation, a pedagogically correct but culturally distant or overly formal response would not count as satisfying for the target student population — yet the benchmark's output schema has no dimension to capture this. The "Tutor Tone" dimension only distinguishes between encouraging, neutral, and offensive [Q90], without capturing whether the warmth and communication style match what a metropolitan Indian child in grades 1–8 would find engaging or motivating.

### 5. Annotation Process

The annotation team consisted of four annotators (two male, two female), all holding at least a post-graduate degree in Computer Science and proficient in English [Q31]. Notably, the paper explicitly does not require annotators to have direct teaching experience, framing the required competence as the ability to judge responses from the perspective of a potential user or student rather than a teacher [Q32]. To ensure quality, the authors opted against public crowdsourcing platforms (Prolific, MTurk) in favor of in-house annotation with rigorous training protocols [Q33], including an interactive training document, oral instructions [Q34], and a structured validation pilot study [Q35, Q36]. In the pilot, all four annotators independently labeled the same eight randomly sampled dialogues across all eight dimensions, yielding 544 annotations per annotator [Q37, Q38], and Fleiss' kappa was 0.65, indicating substantial agreement [Q39]. For the full MRBench annotation, each annotator covered 48 dialogues, with 40 instances double-annotated for inter-annotator agreement calculation [Q56, Q57], achieving an average Cohen's kappa of 0.71 [Q61]. Additional LLM-based annotation was performed using Prometheus2 and Llama-3.1-8B as critic models [Q105], with annotator training phases illustrated in Figures 4 and 5 [Q152, Q153], and the Prometheus2 prompt template based on that model's official guidelines [Q148].

A critical validity gap for the Indian deployment context is the absence of any documented Indian annotators in the team. The four annotators are described only as Computer Science post-graduates fluent in English [Q31], with no mention of nationality, cultural background, or familiarity with Indian educational norms. Given that the deployment's success criterion includes cultural appropriateness and warmth for Indian students (as flagged in the elicitation), the benchmark's ground-truth labels — particularly for tone, human-likeness, and guidance style — may not reflect the judgments of metropolitan Indian students or human tutors acting as co-authors. The paper's decision not to require teaching experience [Q32] further distances the annotators from the perspective of Indian educators who would co-author responses in the deployment. The pilot validation confirmed eight dimensions as sufficient [Q132], but this sufficiency was assessed without any Indian pedagogical stakeholder input, leaving the OC dimension at moderate validity risk for this deployment.

### 6. Evaluation Metrics and Output Modality

The benchmark uses two primary quantitative metrics: the Desired Annotation Match Rate (DAMR), which measures the percentage of responses receiving the desired annotation label for each dimension [Q64], and Annotation Correlation (AC), which uses Pearson's correlation to compare LLM-generated annotations against human gold-standard annotations [Q66, Q138]. Human evaluation is treated as the gold standard [Q68], and results are reported both at the aggregate level [Q67] and separately for Bridge and MathDial data [Q149, Q150]. Key findings include: GPT-4 reveals the answer approximately 47% of the time [Q69], making it more of a question-answering system than a pedagogical tutor [Q70]; Phi3 performs worst overall [Q72, Q101]; Llama-3.1-405B is the top-performing LLM [Q99]; and Novice human responses score poorly on guidance and actionability [Q79, Q80]. LLM-based evaluators (Prometheus2 and Llama-3.1-8B) show predominantly negative correlation with human annotations across pedagogical dimensions, except for human-likeness [Q107], and the paper concludes these LLMs are unreliable as evaluators in this context [Q114, Q123]. Importantly, high human-likeness scores across most LLMs show that superficial fluency is no longer a differentiating factor, shifting the evaluation focus to deeper semantic and pedagogical qualities [Q91].

For the Indian deployment, the DAMR metric is informative about GPT-4's pedagogical tendencies — particularly its high rate of answer revelation [Q69] — which is directly relevant to assessing whether the co-authored GPT-4 + human tutor system would meet the deployment's pedagogical standards. However, DAMR does not capture student satisfaction or culturally appropriate engagement, and the finding that LLM evaluators are unreliable [Q107, Q109] means that automated re-evaluation of the deployed system using these critic models would not be a valid substitute for human judgment grounded in the Indian context.

### 7. Stated Limitations

The authors identify several categories of limitations. First, general NLG metrics (BLEU, BERTScore, DialogRPT) are explicitly rejected as unsuitable because they do not account for pedagogical values and require gold references that are often unavailable [Q4, Q7, Q8]; additionally, multiple valid responses may exist for a given input [Q9], and simple outputs can inflate such scores [Q10]. Second, the eight taxonomy dimensions are treated as independent for annotation purposes [Q30], but the authors acknowledge inherent interdependencies among them in practice [Q116], such as the relationship between answer revelation and actionability [Q85]. Third, the taxonomy is explicitly scoped to mathematics and the student mistake remediation task [Q117], and the authors acknowledge it will need verification and likely adaptation for other tasks (e.g., concept learning) or subjects [Q118, Q130]. Fourth, the benchmark does not measure impact on overall student learning gains — only individual tutor turn appropriateness — which limits understanding of broader educational outcomes [Q120, Q121]. Fifth, LLM-based evaluators are found to be unreliable because they were not trained on pedagogically rich datasets [Q109], and the evaluation prompt experiments are acknowledged as preliminary [Q110]; Prometheus2's lack of training on the taxonomy's dimensions (except human-likeness) further limits its utility [Q108]. Sixth, LLM outputs carry risks of hallucination, factual errors, and propagation of biases — with the paper noting that hallucinations in the educational domain can misguide students [Q124, Q125]. The paper also notes that some Expert responses failed to identify mistakes [Q87] and that all tutors — human and AI — lack some required pedagogical abilities [Q98, Q104].

For the Indian metropolitan deployment, the most consequential stated limitation is the explicit acknowledgment that the taxonomy is not designed for cultural adaptation [Q117, Q118]: it was developed and validated in a context that does not include Indian curriculum, cultural context, or student population. The absence of a satisfaction or learning-gain outcome measure [Q120, Q121] is particularly salient given that the deployment's primary success criterion is student satisfaction — a construct the benchmark does not operationalize at all. The risk of LLM hallucinations misguiding students [Q125] is also especially relevant in the deployment context, where Grade 1–8 students may lack the metacognitive capacity to detect factually incorrect scaffolding.

### 8. Authors and Affiliations

All four authors — Kaushal Kumar Maurya, KV Aditya Srivatsa, Kseniia Petukhova, and Ekaterina Kochmar — are affiliated with the Mohamed bin Zayed University of Artificial Intelligence (MBZUAI) in Abu Dhabi, UAE [Q6]. The research received partial support from Google through the Google Academic Research Award (GARA) 2024, and computational resources were provided by the MBZUAI campus supercomputing center [Q126]. The UAE-based academic affiliation signals that the benchmark was developed outside both the Western context of its source datasets (Bridge and MathDial) and the Indian metropolitan deployment context — meaning no direct institutional familiarity with Indian curriculum standards, NCERT alignment, or Indian classroom communication norms can be inferred from the authorship alone.
