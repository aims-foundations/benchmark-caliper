### 1. Task Taxonomy / Test Case Categories

CrisisLTLSum is organized around two downstream tasks: timeline extraction and timeline summarization [Q4], both operating over local crisis event timelines drawn from four domains — wildfire, local fire, traffic, and storm [Q53, Q122]. The timeline extraction task is framed as a sequential classification problem: given a seed tweet identifying the onset of a crisis event, the system must determine whether each subsequent tweet in a noisy stream belongs to the same evolving event [Q5, Q83]. The timeline summarization task builds on extraction output and requires generating an abstractive paragraph that captures the progression of the event over time [Q6, Q84]. The benchmark explicitly restricts scope to "local" events — those geographically bounded to a specific building, street, or county and typically short in duration [Q12] — and positions itself as the first annotated dataset for this extraction problem [Q11] and the first benchmark for abstractive timeline summarization in the crisis domain on Twitter [Q20].

From a validity standpoint for the Brazilian deployment context, this task taxonomy presents a fundamental ontological mismatch (IO: HIGH). The four crisis types entirely omit large-scale flood events, and the "local" framing is structurally incompatible with multi-municipality flood emergencies of the kind Defesa Civil coordinates. The benchmark's local-event framing [Q12, Q19, Q40] was designed for bounded infrastructure events (a building fire, a traffic accident), not for the extended, geographically diffuse, and socially complex humanitarian crises — river-level progression, dam failures, isolated communities, shelter capacity tracking — that characterize flood response in Rio Grande do Sul. The online clustering logic is explicitly designed around short-duration, location-pinned events [Q44, Q46], which further limits transferability to prolonged flood events that evolve across days or weeks and span dozens of municipalities. The output taxonomy (OO: HIGH) similarly reflects this mismatch: the benchmark's binary extraction-then-summarize pipeline produces single-event narrative paragraphs [Q84], whereas operational Defesa Civil coordination requires outputs structured by geographic unit and surfacing cross-cutting humanitarian need categories (rescue, shelter, supply, road access) that the benchmark's label system does not represent.

### 2. Data Sources and Collection

CrisisLTLSum comprises 1,000 crisis event timelines (10,610 tweets) drawn from the public Twitter stream via a semi-automated cluster-then-refine pipeline [Q1, Q2, Q18, Q27]. Geographic scope was explicitly constrained to seven US states (New York, Illinois, Massachusetts, California, Texas, Florida) and two Australian states (New South Wales and Victoria), selected because they exhibit high frequencies of the four target crisis types [Q123, Q124]. Temporal scope covers 2020 and 2021, with area–domain–time combinations manually selected to maximize event density [Q32]. The 1,000 clusters submitted for annotation were manually curated by the paper's authors themselves, who also identified the seed tweet for each timeline [Q52, Q138]; this means the initial framing of what counts as a "crisis event" reflects the authors' judgments rather than community or domain-expert input.

For the Brazilian deployment (IC: HIGH), the geographic and linguistic provenance of the data is critically misaligned. All source tweets are in English, from US and Australian geographic contexts, and filtered using English crisis keywords [Q31, Q126]. No Brazilian Portuguese text, no references to Brazilian municipalities, bairros, or rivers, and no Brazilian emergency terminology (e.g., 'alagamento', 'ilhado', 'Defesa Civil') appear anywhere in the collection process. The filtering criteria explicitly anchor location detection to a curated list of US and Australian cities and neighborhoods [Q30], meaning the pipeline as described could not be applied without substantial re-engineering to Rio Grande do Sul. The dataset is publicly released [Q8], and a companion statistics table (Table 8) documents domain and split distributions [Q146], but the English/US-Australian provenance remains a hard constraint on content validity for the target deployment.

---

### 3. Data Format and Preprocessing

Input tweets undergo a multi-stage preprocessing pipeline before annotation or model training. Location filtering uses geo-tags, user location fields, and text mentions against a curated city/neighborhood list [Q30]; domain filtering applies manually curated keyword lists with lemmatized and lowercased matching, treating multi-word keywords as bag-of-word constraints rather than exact n-grams [Q129, Q130, Q131]. A pre-trained AllenNLP NER model (CoNLL03-trained) extracts person, location, and organization entities [Q33], supplemented by a BERT-based NER model fine-tuned on Twitter data for location detection specifically [Q35], with OpenStreetMap API used for address normalization [Q36]. Post-processing includes fuzzy-string duplicate removal within clusters [Q136] and manual cluster merging [Q50]. The final dataset is split 70/10/20 (train/dev/test) via stratified sampling on domain and timeline length [Q80, Q81].

Timeline length distributions reveal important structural characteristics: 45% of timelines contain five tweets or fewer [Q73], while 9% contain 26 or more tweets [Q74], and 11% of timelines consist entirely of positive-label tweets while 7% contain none [Q72]. For model training, tweet sequences are constructed by concatenating tweets up to each time step [Q85], formatted as "t0 [SEP] si" for BERT fine-tuning with a 512-token limit [Q86]. Summaries are paired using the two most-agreeing annotators' outputs [Q88, Q90]. From a deployment perspective (IF: MODERATE), the benchmark is entirely text-based, aligning with the text-only nature of the Brazilian deployment; however, the benchmark ingests only Twitter posts, while the Defesa Civil use case also involves Facebook posts, whose longer length, threading structure, and differing informal conventions are not represented in the preprocessing design. The keyword-based domain filtering [Q125, Q126, Q127, Q128] was developed from English-language crisis news and social media, making it non-transferable to Brazilian Portuguese without complete reconstruction.

---

### 4. Label Categories and Output Types

The label ontology for timeline extraction is a binary classification: each tweet is either part of the timeline or not, conditional on three simultaneously required properties — the tweet must be (1) relevant (same event as seed), (2) informative (factual rather than purely opinion), and (3) non-repetitive (introducing new information) [Q58]. Majority vote across three annotators determines the ground-truth label [Q70], yielding 4,303 positive (41%) and 6,307 negative (59%) instances [Q71]. A timeline itself is defined as a chronologically sorted set of posts, each adding new information about an ongoing event [Q3].

For summarization quality, the human evaluation uses a rich multi-dimensional label schema applied on a 1–5 scale across four axes: Coherence (grammaticality and understandability) [Q153, Q154], Accuracy (factual faithfulness to the timeline) [Q155, Q156], Coverage (completeness of important event information) [Q157, Q158], and Overall Quality [Q159, Q160]. This four-axis schema is operationally defined with detailed per-score rubrics [Q154, Q156, Q158, Q160]. From an output ontology perspective (OO: HIGH), the binary extraction labels and free-form summary paragraphs do not map onto the structured need-categories (rescue, shelter, supply, road access) or geographic-unit segmentation that Defesa Civil operational workflows require. The "informative" criterion [Q58] was defined in the context of English-language US crisis events, and its application to Brazilian Portuguese social media — where informal, emotional, or community-support posts may carry operationally critical situational information for humanitarian coordinators — may systematically exclude content that domain experts would consider relevant.

---

### 5. Annotation Process

Annotation was conducted on Amazon Mechanical Turk (MTurk), with tasks divided into domain-specific batches of HITs, each containing three timelines and collected from three workers [Q54, Q55, Q56]. Workers received the seed tweet and chronologically ordered subsequent tweets and were asked to make per-tweet inclusion decisions with reasons for exclusions, then write a concise event-progression summary [Q57, Q59]. Quality control was multi-layered: geographic restriction to countries with predominantly native English speakers (QC1) [Q61], a qualification test requiring annotation of three timelines (QC2) [Q62], expert review of annotator performance against expert labels and manual review of summary quality (QC3) [Q64], and ongoing performance monitoring during the main task (QC4) [Q66]. Compensation was set at $3 per HIT with bonuses targeting an effective rate above $10/hour [Q67, Q68].

Inter-annotator agreement was measured at the timeline level: the average agreement between the two most-agreeing workers per timeline was 90.06% [Q77], and worker-vs.-expert agreement on 20% of timelines reached 91.77% [Q79]. For the human evaluation of summarization quality, five workers per summary rated eight summaries per timeline across the four axes, with a pre-task screening test ensuring comprehension of rubrics [Q106, Q145, Q152]. Two of the three worker summaries per timeline (those from the most-agreeing annotator pair) were used as multi-reference ground truth [Q88, Q95].

For the Brazilian deployment (OC: HIGH), the annotator population represents a fundamental validity threat. Geographic restriction to native-English-speaking countries [Q61] systematically excludes Brazilian Portuguese speakers, meaning no annotator had the cultural, linguistic, or geographic familiarity to judge content relevance for Rio Grande do Sul flood events. The "informative" and "relevant" label definitions [Q58] were operationalized in an English-language US emergency context; applying these criteria to Brazilian Portuguese flood-related social media — where local vocabulary, Gaúcho regional expressions, and Brazilian emergency jargon carry meaning that non-Brazilian annotators could not reliably assess — would produce ground-truth labels misaligned with what Defesa Civil coordinators would judge correct. The inter-operational, multi-role judgment panel that the deployment context requires [per elicitation Q3] has no analog in this annotation design.

### 6. Evaluation Metrics and Output Modality

Timeline extraction is evaluated using average timeline-level accuracy [Q91], with the best baseline model achieving 73.51 against a human-level performance of 88.98, a gap the authors flag as motivating future research [Q96]. Summarization is evaluated using multi-reference ROUGE scores computed via SacreROUGE [Q98, Q100], in both oracle (gold-label input) and predicted-timeline settings [Q101]. A separate human evaluation assessed summaries on the four-axis rubric (Coherence, Accuracy, Coverage, Overall Quality) with five raters per summary across eight summary variants per timeline [Q103, Q104]; human-written summaries scored an average overall quality of 4.12, significantly outperforming model outputs [Q108]. Model training details are fully documented for reproducibility [Q141, Q142, Q143, Q144]. The online clustering similarity metric is also described as a custom combination of entity similarity, geographic distance, and shared hashtags, with tuned hyper-parameters [Q41, Q42].

From a deployment perspective, ROUGE-based evaluation measures lexical overlap against English-language reference summaries written by US-context annotators, which is not a valid proxy for operational usefulness to Brazilian emergency coordinators. The benchmark's evaluation framework provides no mechanism for assessing whether summaries surface the humanitarian need categories (rescue, shelter, supply, road access) or geographic granularity (municipality, bairro) that constitute correctness in the target deployment. The human evaluation's overall-quality axis [Q108] uses a single aggregate score that conflates dimensions that would need to be independently weighted for Defesa Civil use (e.g., geographic accuracy and humanitarian-need coverage are distinct operational priorities that the current rubric merges into "Coverage" [Q157]).

### 7. Stated Limitations

The authors acknowledge a significant performance gap between strong baselines and human performance on both tasks [Q7, Q97], framing this as a research challenge rather than a deployment concern. On the data collection side, they note that the noisy timeline collection process is not comprehensive or exhaustive and is intended to be representative rather than complete [Q117], and that the keyword-based domain filtering approach is similarly non-exhaustive [Q132]. The pipeline is explicitly described as dependent on the accuracy of entity extraction modules — particularly location extraction — and the OpenStreetMap API, making replication for other languages or geographic regions explicitly difficult [Q118, Q119]. The online clustering hyperparameters were tuned heuristically from a small number of experiments [Q43, Q120], and dataset access is further limited by Twitter stream availability and potential post deletion [Q121].

Additional limitations with direct relevance to the Brazilian deployment include: the NER module's failure to extract uncommon or special entities [Q34], which in a Brazilian context would include municipality names, bairros, and river names that standard CoNLL03-trained models would not recognize; the observation that longer timelines yield lower proportions of positive-labeled tweets [Q75], which may affect flood events that evolve over extended periods; and acknowledged model limitations for long timelines due to BERT's 512-token constraint [Q92, Q93]. The authors also note that the post-cluster-merging step only partially compensates for missing entities in preprocessing [Q51], and that the clustering method may fail to associate tweets describing different angles of the same event [Q134]. The combination of these acknowledged limitations — language dependency [Q119], entity extraction sensitivity [Q118], non-exhaustive geographic scope [Q117], and inapplicability to other locations — collectively confirm that the benchmark was not designed with portability to non-English, non-US-Australian crisis contexts in mind, and the authors themselves signal these as barriers to replication [Q119].

The paper lists six authors: Hossein Rajaby Faghihi, Bashar Alhafni, Ke Zhang, Shihao Ran, Joel Tetreault, and Alejandro Jaimes [Q9], affiliated with Michigan State University, New York University Abu Dhabi, and Dataminr, Inc. [Q10]. The acknowledgments section thanks colleagues at Dataminr specifically, alongside feedback from EMNLP reviewers [Q116], indicating that the benchmark was developed in close collaboration with a commercial social media intelligence company. The Dataminr affiliation is deployment-context-relevant: Dataminr is a US-based real-time event detection platform primarily serving English-language markets, which further contextualizes the English-only, US-and-Australia-focused data collection design. No Brazilian, Latin American, or Portuguese-language institutional affiliations are represented among the authors or acknowledgments, which is consistent with the benchmark's lack of coverage of the linguistic, geographic, and operational dimensions relevant to the Rio Grande do Sul deployment context.
